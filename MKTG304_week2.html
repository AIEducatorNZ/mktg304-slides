Engagement Questions

Transition slide introducing the opening engagement questions.
Discussion 1 of 8
Modalities
A client asks you to produce 30 social media captions for a product launch next week. A colleague suggests just running them all through Claude. Is that Automation, Augmentation, or Agency — and is it the right call?
It's Automation as described. The right-call question is where discussion gets interesting: who checks 30 outputs? Is the brief specific enough? Will they be generic? Push students toward the idea that this probably should be Augmentation.
Discussion 2 of 8
Hallucination
An AI drafts a competitive analysis for your client presentation. It includes specific market share figures and cites two industry reports. What do you do before that slide goes to the client?
Looking for: verify every figure independently, check the reports actually exist and say what the AI claims. Specific factual claims — numbers, citations — are exactly where hallucination is most common and most dangerous professionally.
Discussion 3 of 8
Delegation
Your team is about to use AI to develop a brand strategy for a new client. What questions should you be asking before anyone opens a chat window?
Looking for Goal & Task Awareness: What is the actual goal? Which parts require human judgment? What platform is appropriate — data privacy implications of putting client info into a consumer tool? What will the client expect? The key insight: Delegation is a strategic pause, not a preamble.
Discussion 4 of 8
Description
Two students submit AI-assisted campaign proposals. One is generic and could apply to any brand; the other is sharp and specific. Neither student is more creative than the other. What's most likely the actual difference between them?
The difference is upstream — in the quality of context and constraints given in the prompt, not in the output. The generic output means the AI defaulted to the most common version of "campaign proposal" in training data. Also likely: the sharper student iterated rather than accepting the first output.
Discussion 5 of 8
Description–Discernment Loop
You prompt an AI for a media strategy and the output is mediocre. Your first instinct is to try a completely different prompt. What should you actually do first — and why?
Evaluate before rewriting. Is it a prompt-writing problem or an AI capability problem? Rewriting without diagnosing means you might fix the wrong thing. Walk through what evaluation looks like: accuracy, relevance, tone, completeness — then make a targeted change.
Discussion 6 of 8
Discernment
An AI produces a buyer persona for Music Planet. It reads confidently and is well written. What would you actually do before using it — and what specifically are you looking for?
Beyond "read it carefully": Does the demographic data match Music Planet's actual customer base? Are motivations and behaviours plausible or generic? Does it reflect AI bias in assumptions about age, gender, income? What's missing? Could you test it against real customer data? Well-written confidence is exactly when Discernment matters most.
Discussion 7 of 8
Diligence
You use AI heavily to draft a proposal for a real client. You review it, make some edits, and submit it as your work. Is that acceptable — and what would Diligence actually require of you beyond just reviewing it?
Deliberately ambiguous. Yes, it can be acceptable — but Transparency diligence requires disclosure in accordance with context norms. Deployment diligence requires genuine verification, not just a read-through. Core principle: AI assistance does not transfer responsibility. You own the proposal regardless of how it was produced.
Discussion 8 of 8
Client Intelligence Brief
Every time you begin a new AI conversation, the model starts with little or no knowledge of the client. Imagine you could create one canonical document to include with every client prompt — giving the AI the context it needs to produce specific, relevant, and useful work rather than generic answers.

What should we call this document, and what information would it need to contain?
Use this as a bridge into the practical prompting work. The name we want students to arrive at is Client Intelligence Brief: a canonical client document that can be pasted into future AI prompts so the model has enough context to produce specific, relevant work. Ideas that should ideally surface during discussion: - Client background, products, services, and business model. - Market context and relevant industry trends. - Target markets and buyer personas. - Competitors and the client's positioning. - Value proposition and key differentiators. - Brand identity, tone of voice, and key messaging. - Current marketing channels and digital presence. - Business and marketing objectives. - Challenges, constraints, and opportunities. - Relevant research, evidence, and source material. - Important facts the AI must not invent or assume. - Known gaps or questions requiring further research. Teaching point: this document is broader than a market research report. It includes market intelligence, brand context, strategic priorities, evidence, constraints, and assumptions. The stronger the Client Intelligence Brief, the less generic the AI output will be.

Two Things the Certification Didn't Cover

Positional effects
Models pay most attention to the beginning and end of a prompt. Critical instructions buried in the middle get lost.

Liu et al. (2023) — "Lost in the Middle"
Practical rule: Put your context and role first. Put your specific task instruction immediately after any document you paste in — never before it. This maps directly onto the CRAFT framework in Part 2.
Temperature
Controls randomness. The same prompt will not always give the same answer — and that is by design.

Low temp = consistent, predictable.
High temp = more creative variation.

Consumer tools don't expose this directly, but understanding it explains why regenerating a response is sometimes valid — and why you should never treat the first output as definitive.
Everything else — hallucination, context windows, knowledge cutoffs, stochasticity — you covered in your certification. Apply that knowledge throughout today.
Keep this brief - 3 minutes maximum. Students know the fundamentals. These two points are the ones with the most direct practical impact on prompt quality that the certification doesn't specifically name. The callout is a respectful acknowledgement that they did the work - don't re-teach what they already know.

Deployment speed has outpaced governance maturity. The gap is widening, not closing.

12

(METR, 2025)
D or below
Every major AI company scored D or below

Anthropic C+ · OpenAI C · Google C− · xAI & Meta D  (SaferAI / Future of Life Institute, 2025)
Source: AI Research Report, Section 1. The grading used a letter-grade scale across two independent 2025 studies. Progress from 3 to 12 companies is real - but 12 is still a fraction of the industry. Keep this slide to 2 minutes and move into the harms.

The Harms That Are Already Here

1
Bias & Discrimination
AI trained on historical data reproduces existing inequalities. Facial recognition misidentifies people of colour at significantly higher rates (NIST, 2019). Amazon scrapped its own recruiting tool after it downgraded CVs containing the word "women's." These are not edge cases.
2
Copyright & IP
Models trained on scraped content without permission. The New York Times sued OpenAI and Microsoft in 2023. Getty Images sued Stability AI. For marketing professionals: AI-generated creative work may carry legal exposure not yet fully understood.
3
Synthetic Media
AI-generated content is being used to fabricate news, impersonate real people, and produce fake reviews at scale. Regulators in multiple jurisdictions are beginning to require disclosure of AI-generated advertising material.
These harms are documented, ongoing, and in several cases already subject to litigation and regulatory action.
Keep to 3 minutes. Ask verbally: "If you used AI to generate a product image for a client campaign, do you think you'd need to disclose that?" There's no clean NZ-specific legal answer yet — that ambiguity is the point.

Case Study: The OpenAI Safety Team Exodus

May 2024
Ilya Sutskever (co-founder) departs. Jan Leike resigns as Superalignment co-leader. Team subsequently disbanded.
May–Oct 2024
~50% of safety-focused staff gone. Wave of departures includes Kokotajlo, O'Keefe, Murati, McGrew, Zoph.
Feb 2026
OpenAI's Mission Alignment team dissolved — third safety team disbanded in under two years. Same month: Anthropic drops its Responsible Scaling Policy pledge under commercial pressure.
"Create a safety team. Dissolve it. Create another. Dissolve it."
This is a structural story, not a personnel story.
Keep to 3 minutes. The key insight: safety commitments are negotiable under commercial pressure — at every major lab, not just OpenAI. The Anthropic RSP detail in February 2026 lands harder when students are using Claude. Ask verbally: "What does repeated disbanding of safety teams tell you about the incentive structures?" Part 1 — Security & Privacy

The Attack Surface Is Language

LLM security risks differ from traditional software. The vulnerability is natural language itself.
#1
Prompt injection — OWASP Top 10
for LLM Applications (2025)
43%
of AI platform users do so via personal,
unmanaged accounts (LayerX Security, 2025)
Two risk types: prompt injection and data leakage through unmanaged tool use outside organisational IT oversight.
2 minutes. The 43% figure is the one that lands — nearly half of employees using AI at work are doing so on personal accounts with no organisational oversight. That's the data leakage risk in one number.
Three Rules — Starting Now
1
Treat every prompt as potentially permanent and potentially public.
2
Never enter client data, personal data, or confidential information into a consumer AI tool.
3
Default settings on ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini allow conversations to be used for model training. Most users never change this.
2 minutes. Rule 3 is the one that surprises students most. Note: for the Music Planet exercise, we are not sharing any real confidential client data, so consumer tools are fine for this session.

The Numbers

Microsoft emissions rise year-on-year in FY25 as AI infrastructure expanded
New Zealand context: 85.5% of NZ electricity is renewable (2024) — but 64% of the 1,780 MW now in NZ’s new load pipeline is data centres. The issue is less today’s average emissions than where future demand lands.
Efficiency is real — Google improved TPU compute carbon intensity threefold across five generations. But demand is growing faster than efficiency can offset. That is the rebound effect.
3 minutes. The inference figure (80–90%) is the counterintuitive point — the environmental cost is driven by daily queries, not the headline training runs. The NZ context is new and relevant to this audience: a renewable grid does not mean zero impact when a large pipeline of new data-centre demand is coming. Microsoft’s 25% year-on-year rise is from their FY25 report, which they did not hide — worth noting that candour exists.

Water: What We Know and What We Don’t

Withdrawal vs Consumption
Withdrawal — water taken from a source; some is returned.

Consumption — water not returned, usually because it evaporates through cooling towers.

These are not the same thing, and most headlines do not tell you which one they mean.
The “Bottle of Water” Claim
The famous GPT-3 estimate — 500 mL per 10–50 responses — was one model, one Microsoft data centre, specific cooling assumptions, U.S. electricity mix.

It is not a universal figure. The same researchers say WUE varies by location, season, load, and cooling design.
The honest answer: AI-specific water disclosure is still weak across the industry. No standardised reporting exists — and that data gap is itself information worth knowing.
The Hidden Water Cost
On-site cooling is only part of the picture. Generating the electricity a data centre uses also consumes water — at the power station, off-site.

In some regions that indirect cost exceeds the on-site figure entirely.
Amazon — 2025 Sustainability Report
Of all water withdrawn by Amazon’s data centres globally:
48%
came from low water-stress regions
22%
came from high or extremely high water-stress regions
Same company. Very different local impact depending on where the server is.
3 minutes. Key teaching points: (1) withdrawal and consumption are different — most headlines conflate them; (2) the 500mL figure is assumption-dependent, not universal; (3) off-site water from electricity generation often exceeds the on-site figure; (4) the Amazon split shows the same company has very different local impacts by geography. Note: 48% and 22% do not add to 100% — the remainder falls in medium-stress regions. The honest answer card connects to Discernment: this is exactly the kind of confident-sounding claim that falls apart when you interrogate the assumptions.

What This Means for Marketers

Environmental intensity of common tasks
Lower — one well-planned text prompt, bounded summarisation
Moderate — repeated regeneration of similar text, long-document analysis
Higher — high-resolution image generation, short-form video, large-scale personalisation at campaign scale
Highly variable — always-on chatbots, agentic workflows with hidden model calls
One careful prompt ≠ 80 ad variants + 40 images + 10 videos + an always-on agent. Volume, modality, and automation all increase impact.
Before — During — After
Before
Does this task need AI? Is a smaller or non-generative tool adequate? Is text enough?
During
Plan before prompting. Limit near-duplicate retries. Revise a useful draft rather than starting over.
After
Stop when marginal value drops. Read provider sustainability claims with healthy scepticism. Disclose AI use honestly.
3 minutes. The intensity gradient should feel familiar — it maps directly onto decisions students will make in A3 and A4. The rebound point is the one worth lingering on: the environmental risk for marketing teams isn’t the single prompt, it’s the workflow that generates 80 ad variants, 20 synthetic personas, 40 images, and then runs an always-on agent to keep refining them. The before/during/after framework is from the report and is deliberately simple — three questions, not a checklist of twelve. The goal is not abstinence, it is informed professional judgment. Source: The Hidden Footprint of Helpful Machines, Tables 3 and 5.

From the Certification to Practice

You already know the six prompting techniques. What the certification doesn't give you is a framework for combining them — and a way to make the AI critique its own output before you accept it.

That's what the next 40 minutes are for.
CRAFT framework
Meta-prompting
Live exercise → Music Planet report
Verbal bridge: "You've certified in the techniques. Context, examples, constraints, decompose, chain of thought, role assignment — you know what these are. The question is how you deploy them together, deliberately, for a specific purpose. That's CRAFT." Keep this slide to 60 seconds and move straight in.

The CRAFT Framework

C
Context
The background the model needs. Client, industry, problem, current state.
R
Role
The expert persona it should adopt. Sets tone, depth, and vocabulary.
A
Action
The specific task. Verb-led. Unambiguous. Scoped.
F
Format
Structure, length, required elements, excluded elements.
T
Target Audience
Who the output is ultimately for. Shapes vocabulary and assumed knowledge.
Context comes first because it is the single element most likely to transform a generic response into a genuinely useful one. If removing your Context block would leave an adequate response, you haven't provided enough context.
5 minutes. Walk through each letter with one sentence. Emphasise that Context is the most commonly under-specified element — and the one with the biggest impact. The test at the bottom is worth saying aloud.

CRAFT in Action: Music Planet

<context>
Music Planet is a family-owned NZ music retailer founded in 1980.
It operates showrooms in Auckland, Wellington, Christchurch, Hamilton,
and Dunedin, plus a nationwide online store. Product range spans
instruments, equipment, sheet music, and lessons. Key challenge:
competing against online-only retailers and global platforms
while leveraging its physical presence and 40+ years of expertise.
</context>

<role>
You are a senior digital marketing strategist with expertise in
NZ retail and omnichannel marketing for specialist consumer brands.
</role>

<action>
Produce a comprehensive market research report for Music Planet
that will be used as context for all future AI-assisted
marketing tasks throughout this course.
</action>

<format>
1. Company overview and positioning
2. Target customer segments (2–3, with demographics and behaviours)
3. Competitive landscape (direct and indirect competitors)
4. Current digital presence assessment
5. Key marketing challenges and opportunities
6. Brand voice and tone guidelines
</format>

<target_audience>
University marketing students using this as a working reference
document for AI-assisted campaign work. Assume marketing
knowledge but no prior familiarity with Music Planet.
</target_audience>
Context Specific enough to be Music Planet — not generic "music retailer." The key challenge is named.
Role NZ retail specialism matters. Generic "marketing expert" would produce generic output.
Action States the purpose of the document — "for all future AI-assisted marketing tasks" — which shapes what the AI includes.
Format Six numbered sections. The AI cannot misunderstand the structure.
Target Audience Calibrates depth and assumed knowledge precisely.
5 minutes. Walk through the annotations column rather than reading the prompt aloud. The point is how each element does specific work — not just that it's long. You can run this live if internet is available.

Meta-CRAFT: Use the Prompt Builder

Open the linked Meta-CRAFT prompt, paste it into Claude, ChatGPT, or Gemini, and follow the instructions.
How it works
The Meta-CRAFT prompt asks structured questions about your task, audience, context, role, constraints, and output format. It then produces a stronger prompt that you can copy, paste, and execute.
Why use it?
It helps you avoid vague, under-specified prompts. Instead of trying to craft the perfect prompt from scratch, you use the tool to build one deliberately.

Using the Meta-CRAFT Prompt
