What the Model Is Actually Doing

Not thinking. Not understanding. Pattern-matching at scale.
📱 The Analogy
Phone keyboard autocomplete — but trained on hundreds of billions of tokens of human text.

Example: "marketing" → tokens "market" + "ing". The sentence "Write me a marketing plan" is ~7 tokens. GPT-4 processed ~300 trillion tokens of training data — roughly every word ever published on the English-language internet, multiple times over.
🎲 Tokens, Not Words
Models process tokens (~3–4 characters each). Small wording changes shift the probability distribution.

Why it matters: Changing a single word can shift the model's probability distribution enough to produce a completely different output. "Summarise" and "analyse" return different responses to the same document — not because the model understands the difference, but because those words co-occur with different patterns in training data.
⚡ The Consequence
You are not commanding a machine. You are nudging a probability distribution. Every word choice matters.
Key implication: The same prompt can give different results each time — and small changes in phrasing can produce dramatically different outputs.
Source: Prompt Engineering Guide, Section 2. Models use transformer-based architectures where every token attends to every other token (self-attention). They predict the statistically most likely continuation of your input — not the "correct" answer. This probabilistic nature is fundamental, not a bug. Ask students: "Why does this matter for how you write prompts?" Expected answer: because precision in language directly shapes the output. Classroom prompt: Ask students: if you typed the same prompt into ChatGPT ten times right now, would you get the same answer each time? Take a show of hands. Then explain why the answer is no — and why that is a design feature, not a flaw. Temperature controls the degree of randomness, and some randomness is desirable for creative tasks. The problem is that most students don't know this is happening, so they accept the first output as if it were definitive.

Context, Position & Temperature

1
Context Window
The model's working memory. What falls outside it is forgotten entirely. Long prompts mean earlier content competes for attention.

Claude's context window is 200,000 tokens (~150,000 words — a full novel). GPT-4o's is 128,000 tokens. In practice, you can paste an entire report and ask questions about it. But the model does not read it the way a human would — it processes the entire window simultaneously, and content in the middle receives systematically less attention than content at the start or end.
2
Primacy & Recency Bias
Models pay most attention to the beginning and end of a prompt. Critical instructions buried in the middle get lost.
Liu et al., 2023 — "Lost in the Middle"
Practical rule: If you are writing a long prompt with a system role, background context, and a task instruction, always restate the most critical part of your task instruction immediately before the content you want analysed. Do not assume the model remembers what you asked at the top of a 2,000-word prompt by the time it reaches the bottom.
3
Temperature
Controls randomness. Low temp = consistent, predictable. High temp = more creative variation. Same prompt ≠ same answer.

Temp 0 = always picks the single most probable next token. Deterministic, but can feel flat. Temp 1 (default) = samples probabilistically, producing natural variation. Temp 2 = high randomness, useful for brainstorming but often incoherent. Consumer tools don't expose this directly — API users can set it precisely.
Practical takeaway: Put your most important instructions at the start and immediately before the task. Never bury them in the middle.
Source: Prompt Engineering Guide, Section 2. Liu et al. (2023) documented the "lost in the middle" phenomenon in a formal study — models reliably underperform on information placed in the centre of long context windows. Temperature is not usually directly controlled in ChatGPT/Claude.ai/Gemini consumer tools, but understanding it explains why regenerating a response is sometimes valid. All three major providers (Anthropic, OpenAI, Google) recommend keeping temperature at default for most tasks. The context window concept is one of the most practically important things in this section. A common student error is pasting a 10-page document into a prompt and then asking a question at the very top, followed by the document. The model processes the question, then reads through the document, and by the time it finishes, the primacy of the question has faded. These two rules are not contradictory — they apply to different parts of the prompt. Role and system instructions should go at the very start. When you are pasting a long document into a prompt, the specific question or task instruction should be restated immediately after the document, not buried before it. Always put your question or instruction after the document, not before it — or restate it at the end. This is directly supported by Liu et al. (2023) and by Google's own Gemini documentation, which explicitly recommends placing questions after documents.
THE FUNDAMENTAL POINT
"You are not commanding a machine.
You are communicating with a probability engine."
The better you brief it — the clearer the context, the sharper the instruction — the better the prediction.
What this means in practice: hallucinations — confidently wrong answers — are not bugs. They are the predictable output of a system that generates the most statistically likely continuation of your input, without any mechanism for verifying whether that continuation is true. Every LLM hallucinates. The question is how often, and whether you catch it.

This is why the next section matters. Understanding how these tools work at a technical level is inseparable from understanding the ethical and professional risks they create.

The Harms That Are Already Here

1
Bias & Discrimination
AI systems trained on historical data reproduce and amplify existing inequalities. Facial recognition misidentifies people of colour at significantly higher error rates (NIST, 2019). Hiring algorithms have been shown to systematically disadvantage women — Amazon scrapped its own recruiting tool after it downgraded CVs containing the word "women's." Credit scoring and content moderation systems encode similar patterns. These are not edge cases. They are the predictable output of training on biased data.
2
Copyright & Intellectual Property
Generative AI models are trained on scraped web content, often without permission or compensation to the original creators. The New York Times sued OpenAI and Microsoft in 2023. Getty Images sued Stability AI. These cases are unresolved. For marketing professionals: AI-generated copy, images, and creative work may carry legal exposure that is not yet fully understood.
3
Synthetic Media & Misinformation
AI-generated images, video, and text are already being used to fabricate news, impersonate real people, and produce fake product reviews at scale. The marketing implications are direct: synthetic content blurs the line between authentic and manufactured, and regulators in multiple jurisdictions are beginning to require disclosure of AI-generated advertising material.
Framing: These harms are not hypothetical. They are documented, ongoing, and in several cases already subject to litigation and regulatory action.

Governance Is Catching Up — Slowly

✓ Some progress
The EU AI Act (in force August 2024) is the world's first comprehensive AI legal framework. It explicitly prohibits AI systems that cause harm through subliminal manipulation, social scoring, or real-time biometric surveillance in public spaces. High-risk applications — including AI used in hiring, credit, and education — face mandatory transparency and human oversight requirements.
~ Incomplete
16 AI companies committed to publishing frontier safety policies at the Seoul Summit (May 2024). By December 2025, 12 had delivered. Progress is real but partial.
✗ Regression
The US revoked its AI safety executive order in January 2025, reframing federal AI policy around competitive dominance rather than harm reduction. Anthropic dropped its Responsible Scaling Policy pledge in February 2026 under commercial pressure.
The gap that matters for practitioners: Regulation tells you the floor. It does not tell you what is ethical. Many documented harms — biased outputs, synthetic media, opaque automated decisions — are legal in most jurisdictions. Professional judgment is required where law does not yet reach.

Section 4 — Prompting

The Five Core Principles

Endorsed by OpenAI, Google, and Anthropic. Apply across all models and all tasks.
1
Be Clear & Specific
Vague prompts produce vague outputs — not because the model is lazy, but because ambiguity expands the probability space and the model fills it with the most generic response. Specify what you want, for whom, in what format, at what length, and in what tone. Every variable you leave unspecified is a decision you are delegating to the model.
2
Provide Context
The model knows nothing about your situation unless you tell it. It cannot see your brief, your client, your previous work, or your constraints. Context is the information the model needs to interpret your request correctly — who you are, who the output is for, what has already happened, and what the stakes are. Without it, the model defaults to the most generic version of your task.
3
Specify the Format
If you do not specify a format, the model will choose one — and its default is usually not what you need. State length (word count or number of points), structure (table, bullet list, paragraphs), tone (formal, conversational, persuasive), and what to include or exclude. A response in the wrong format wastes more time than a response with the wrong content, because format errors are invisible until you try to use the output.
4
Use Affirmative Instructions
Tell the model what to do, not what to avoid. "Write in plain English for a non-specialist audience" outperforms "Don't use jargon" — empirically, not just intuitively. Bsharat et al. (2023) tested this across GPT-3.5, GPT-4, and LLaMA and found affirmative instructions consistently produced better outputs. Negative instructions create ambiguity; affirmative instructions reduce it.
5
Iterate Systematically
No prompt is perfect on the first attempt. The key discipline is to change one variable at a time so you can attribute what improved. If you rewrite the entire prompt when something is wrong, you will not know what fixed it — and you will repeat the same mistakes in a new prompt. Define what a good output looks like before you start iterating, otherwise you cannot tell whether you are making progress.
Source: Prompt Engineering Guide, Section 3. All five principles are drawn from primary documentation by OpenAI, Google Gemini, and Anthropic. Principle 4 is grounded in empirical research: Bsharat et al. (2023), "Principled Instructions Are All You Need for Questioning LLaMA-1/2, GPT-3.5/4," found that affirmative instructions outperform negative ones consistently. Principle 5 is often overlooked — Anthropic guidance says to define success criteria before you begin iterating, otherwise you cannot tell whether changes are improvements. These five principles are not arbitrary — they are each a response to a specific, predictable failure mode. Principle 1 addresses ambiguity. Principle 2 addresses missing context. Principle 3 addresses format mismatch. Principle 4 addresses instructional clarity. Principle 5 addresses random trial-and-error. When a student's prompt produces a bad output, you can almost always trace it to a failure on one of these five dimensions. Teaching students to diagnose before they rewrite is the most transferable skill in this section.

Principles in Practice: Weak vs Strong

✗ Weak Prompt
"Write a marketing email for Hnry."
No role. No audience. No platform. No tone. No length. No constraints. Output will be generic and likely unusable.
✓ Strong Prompt
You are a senior email copywriter for B2C fintech products in NZ.
Write a 200-word promotional email for Hnry's new GST auto-filing feature.
Audience: sole traders aged 28–45. Tone: conversational, reassuring.
Include one specific pain point (tax filing stress).
End with a CTA to the app. No jargon. No "revolutionary."
Role ✓ · Audience ✓ · Format ✓ · Tone ✓ · Constraints ✓ · Exclusions ✓
Breaking it down:
  • "You are a senior email copywriter for B2C fintech products in NZ" — Role: activates domain-specific tone and vocabulary, sets expertise level
  • "200-word promotional email" — Format: length and document type specified upfront
  • "Audience: sole traders aged 28–45" — Context: who this is for shapes word choice and assumed knowledge
  • "Tone: conversational, reassuring" — Format: overrides the model's default (which for a "promotional email" would likely be salesy)
  • "Include one specific pain point" — Constraint: required element, prevents generic output
  • "End with a CTA to the app" — Constraint: required element, shapes structure
  • "No jargon. No 'revolutionary.'" — Exclusion: affirmative constraints that close off the most common failure modes
Key message: The difference in output quality is the difference between useful and generic. Every one of OpenAI's five format variables — context, outcome, length, format, style — is worth specifying.
Source: Prompt Engineering Guide, Section 3.1 and 3.3. OpenAI's specific guidance is to "be specific about the context, outcome, length, format, and style." The strong prompt example here incorporates the Hnry case study context students will use in assessments — intentional. You can live-demonstrate this: type both prompts into a tool and show the output difference in real time. Walk through this annotation live rather than just showing the before and after. The point is not that the strong prompt is longer — it is that every additional element is doing specific work. Students often think good prompting means writing more. It means writing precisely. A 300-word prompt that is vague in the wrong places will underperform a 100-word prompt that is precise in the right ones. Ask students to identify which of the five principles each annotation maps to — it is a good comprehension check before Exercise 1.

Zero-Shot & Few-Shot Prompting

Zero-Shot
No examples — relies entirely on training. Works well for well-defined, familiar tasks. Appropriate when the task is unambiguous and the model's default behaviour is likely adequate.
Classify the tone of this review as: positive, negative, or neutral.
Review: "Delivery was fast but the product was underwhelming."
Few-Shot
Provide worked examples before the task. Google: "Prompts without few-shot examples are likely to be less effective."

Rules for few-shot design:
  • Use 2–5 examples (too many = overfitting, where the model mimics edge cases rather than the general pattern)
  • Consistent formatting across all examples
  • Diverse examples covering expected variation
  • Most representative example last (recency bias)
  • Show positive patterns, not anti-patterns
Sources: Prompt Engineering Guide §4.1–4.2 · Google Gemini Documentation · Hugging Face Advanced Prompting Guide
Source: Prompt Engineering Guide, Sections 4.1 and 4.2. Anthropic identifies multi-shot prompting as "one of the highest-impact techniques available to users." The recency bias point is directly connected to slide 4 (positional effects) — good opportunity to callback. The "overfitting" risk with too many examples is real: the model begins to match edge-case patterns in the examples rather than generalising from them.

Chain-of-Thought & Role-Based Prompting

Chain-of-Thought (Wei et al., 2022)
Instruct the model to reason step by step before answering. Makes reasoning transparent and self-correcting.

Simple trigger:
"Recommend whether our brand should invest in TikTok advertising.
Think through this step by step before giving your recommendation."
For complex tasks: specify reasoning steps explicitly (Step 1: identify competitors. Step 2: summarise value propositions. Step 3: identify market gap. Step 4: recommend strategy.)
Role-Based
Specificity is everything. Precision activates the right knowledge domain.

✗ "You are a marketing expert."
✓ "You are a senior brand strategist with 10 years' B2C experience in Asia-Pacific FMCG, specialising in customer acquisition for subscription products targeting self-employed professionals."
Note: role assignment adjusts style and expertise emphasis, not model values or safety guidelines.
Source: Prompt Engineering Guide, Sections 4.3 and 4.4. Chain-of-thought formally described by Wei et al. (2022) in "Chain-of-thought prompting elicits reasoning in large language models" (NeurIPS 2022). It "extends the computational graph" of the response — giving the model more steps to self-correct. For Explicit Step CoT, specifying steps before asking for a conclusion is particularly valuable for market analysis tasks in the Proposal Defence. Role prompting effectiveness varies across models and tasks — all three providers note this caveat.

Constraint-Based Prompting

The most neglected technique. Constraints are why outputs stop feeling generic.
Example: LinkedIn post for Hnry
Write a LinkedIn post announcing Hnry's
new GST auto-filing feature.

Constraints:
- Maximum 150 words
- Audience: sole traders & freelancers in NZ
(not accountants)
- Tone: conversational and reassuring, not salesy
- Must include one specific pain point
(e.g., tax filing stress)
- Must end with a question to encourage engagement
- Do not use: "revolutionary" or "game-changing"
Constraint types
Length · Audience · Platform · Tone
Restrictions (no X, no Y) · Required elements

Affirmative > negative: "Use plain language" works better than "Don't use jargon."
Without constraints, the model defaults to the most common version of the task — which is the most generic version.
Source: Prompt Engineering Guide, Section 4.5. The LinkedIn post example comes directly from the guide (Section 4.5). Constraint-based prompting is often combined with role-based and few-shot for maximum effect. The key insight: constraints are not restrictions that limit the model — they are instructions that focus the model on what actually matters for your use case. The preference for affirmative constraints is backed by Bsharat et al. (2023) — same research cited in Principle 4 on slide 19.

Technique in Practice

Choose one technique: chain-of-thought, few-shot, or role-based. Apply it to this prompt:
"Recommend whether Hnry should invest in TikTok advertising in New Zealand."
  • Apply your chosen technique and run the prompt on your device.
  • Find someone who used a different technique. Compare your outputs side by side.
💬 Debrief question: What difference did your technique choice make to the response?
This exercise is designed to make technique choice tangible. Students using chain-of-thought will get a more reasoned, step-by-step analysis. Students using role-based will get a response pitched at a specific expertise level. Few-shot users will need to think about what examples to include — likely previous marketing decisions or platform comparisons. Debrief: surface the fact that different techniques serve different purposes. Connect to Technique Selection Guide in the Prompt Engineering Guide (Section 10). Bridge into CRAFT: "Now you know what to do and how to do it — CRAFT gives you a framework to do it consistently."

Introducing CRAFT

C
Context
The background the model needs. Client, industry, problem, current state, constraints.
R
Role
The expert persona it should adopt. Sets tone, depth, vocabulary, and perspective.
A
Action
The specific task. A clear, verb-led instruction: write, analyse, draft, identify.
F
Format
Structure, length, tone, required elements, excluded elements.
T
Target Audience
Who the output is ultimately for. Shapes vocabulary and assumed knowledge.
Why this order matters: Context comes first because it is the single element most likely to transform a generic response into a genuinely useful one. Without context, even a perfectly specified Role, Action, Format, and Target Audience will produce output that is technically correct but situationally wrong. Role comes second because it shapes the interpretive lens for everything that follows. Action is the task itself — precise, verb-led, unambiguous. Format specifies the deliverable. Target Audience closes the loop by reminding the model who ultimately needs to understand the output.
"If removing your Context block would leave an adequate response, you haven't provided enough context."
Source: Prompt Engineering Guide, Section 6. Context is placed first in CRAFT deliberately — it is the most important component and the most commonly under-specified. The test quoted on the slide is from the guide's "Why Context is the Most Important Element" section. For the Proposal Defence, examiners will assess whether students used AI critically and effectively — a well-constructed CRAFT prompt is direct evidence of deliberate, purposeful engagement. Ask: "What would you include in the Context block for a prompt about Hnry's competitive position in Australia?" CRAFT is not magic — it is a checklist that forces you to make explicit decisions you would otherwise leave implicit. The reason most student prompts underperform is not that they are poorly written — it is that they are incomplete. CRAFT makes incompleteness visible. A student who works through all five components before running a prompt is doing something qualitatively different from a student who types a question. They are briefing a colleague, not searching Google. That distinction is worth making explicitly here before the exercise.

CRAFT in Action: The Hnry Prompt

<context>
You are helping develop a digital marketing strategy for Hnry, a Wellington-based fintech
that automates tax and invoicing for sole traders in NZ, Australia, and the UK. Hnry operates
in a competitive market including traditional accountants and other freelance finance tools.
Primary growth challenge: low brand awareness among sole traders who manage their own tax via IRD.
</context>

<role>
You are a senior digital marketing strategist with 10+ years' experience in B2C fintech marketing
across Asia-Pacific. You specialise in customer acquisition for subscription products targeting self-employed professionals.
</role>

<action>
Develop a 90-day digital marketing plan to increase Hnry's brand awareness among NZ sole traders aged 25–45.
Prioritise two to three channels and include specific KPIs for each.
</action>

<format>
1. Strategic rationale (2–3 paragraphs)
2. Recommended channels (table: channel, rationale, 90-day target KPI)
3. Top three content themes with brief justification
4. One key risk and mitigation strategy
</format>

<target_audience>
This plan will be presented to Hnry's Head of Marketing and a junior marketing team.
Assume familiarity with digital marketing terminology but not with NZ-specific tax context.
</target_audience>
<context> Sets the scene — client, market, problem, growth challenge. Four sentences that change everything.
<role> Specialist expertise + geography + product type. Three specifics that activate the right knowledge domain.
<action> One clear task. Verb-led. Scoped (90 days, two to three channels, specific KPIs). Nothing ambiguous.
<format> Four-part deliverable. Numbered. Each section named. The model cannot misunderstand what is expected.
<target_audience> Shapes vocabulary and assumed knowledge. 'Familiar with digital marketing but not NZ tax context' is a precise, useful instruction.
Source: Prompt Engineering Guide, Section 6 "CRAFT in Action: Full Example." This prompt is reproduced directly from the guide. Note: XML tags are particularly effective with Claude (as stated in Anthropic's documentation and Section 7.2 of the guide) — they act as semantic containers that signal to the model what each section contains. The MKTG 304 application note in the guide states: "In your Proposal Defence, you will be asked to explain your AI usage, including the prompts you used. Examiners will assess whether you used AI critically and effectively." A CRAFT prompt like this is strong evidence of deliberate use. The reason XML tags work particularly well with Claude is that they act as semantic containers — they signal to the model not just what each section says, but what kind of information it contains. This reduces the chance the model confuses background context with an instruction, or treats a constraint as an example. You can run this prompt live during the session if you have internet access — the output quality difference between the CRAFT version and a zero-shot version of the same task ('write a 90-day marketing plan for Hnry') is dramatic enough to be its own teaching moment. If you do this live, ask students to evaluate the output against the Format specification: does it have all four sections? Are the KPIs specific? This is also a preview of how the Proposal Defence examiners will evaluate AI use — not whether AI was used, but whether it was used with deliberate structure and critical judgement.

Advanced Techniques

Claude / XML
Structured XML Tags
Use semantic containers for complex prompts: <context>, <role>, <task>, <constraints>

Particularly effective with Claude — reduces ambiguity and makes multi-part prompts far easier for the model to parse correctly.
Any Model
Prompt Chaining
Break complex tasks into a sequence where each output feeds the next. Validate each step before proceeding.

Example: Research (Step 1) → Ideation (Step 2) → Prioritisation (Step 3) → Production (Step 4).

Reduces per-prompt complexity. Errors are easier to identify and fix.
Quality
Meta-Prompting
Ask the model to critique and revise its own output against explicit criteria before you accept it.

"After writing your draft, review it against these criteria: Does it target the right audience? Is it achievable? Have you addressed the main competitor risk? Revise if it fails on any criterion."
Source: Prompt Engineering Guide, Sections 5.1–5.3. XML tags are explicitly recommended by Anthropic — "more so than with other models" (Section 7.2). Prompt chaining example in Section 5.2 demonstrates a four-step content strategy development chain (research → ideation → prioritisation → production). Meta-prompting is described as "a powerful technique for tasks where quality matters more than speed, and where a human reviewer might not have the domain expertise to catch subtle errors." All three techniques are directly applicable to the Proposal Defence workflow.
