Influencer marketing

What it is, how it works, and how to apply it.
Transition into Section 1. This section is conceptual and discussion-based — it is shorter than the Rival IQ working session but sets up the analytical vocabulary students will need when they assess Hnry's social media presence and consider influencer strategy as part of their audit. Before advancing, confirm that students have their notes open. There are six slides in this section before the activity.
Influencer marketing

What is influencer marketing?

Influencer marketing is the practice of partnering with individuals who have an established audience and credibility in a niche to promote a brand, product, or service.
Unlike celebrity endorsement
Built on perceived authenticity and audience trust, not fame. The influencer has a direct, ongoing relationship with their audience.
Unlike paid advertising
The message is delivered through the creator's own voice and format — not a brand-controlled placement.
The core mechanism
Borrowed credibility. The audience trusts the influencer; that trust transfers to the brand.
The borrowed credibility framing is the most important concept on this slide. Students often describe influencer marketing as "when someone with followers promotes a product," which is accurate but not analytical. The mechanism matters: why does it work? Because the influencer has established trust with their audience, and the act of endorsement transfers a portion of that trust to the brand. This is why authenticity is so commercially valuable — and why forced or over-scripted partnerships tend to underperform. Ask students: "What makes an endorsement feel authentic versus commercial?" before advancing.
Influencer marketing

The influencer landscape

Tier Follower range Key characteristics
Nano 1K–10K High engagement, niche audiences, low cost, often willing to work for product
Micro 10K–100K Strong engagement, more defined niche, cost-effective for targeted campaigns
Macro 100K–1M Broader reach, more professional, higher cost, engagement begins to dilute
Mega / Celebrity 1M+ Mass reach, lowest engagement rates, highest cost and brand risk
Key trade-off: As reach increases, authenticity and engagement rates typically decline.
The follower count benchmarks on this slide are widely used but not universal — they vary by platform and source. Frame them as useful working approximations rather than definitive thresholds. The key teaching point is the trade-off: as reach scales up, the parasocial intimacy that drives engagement tends to dilute. A mega-influencer with five million followers is closer to a TV commercial than a trusted peer recommendation. This is why nano and micro influencers often outperform on engagement rate metrics, even if they reach fewer people in absolute terms. Ask students: "For a brand like Hnry targeting sole traders and freelancers, what tier would you expect to perform best and why?" Hold the answer until Slide 9.
Influencer marketing

Selecting the right influencer

Three selection criteria
  • Audience fit — does their audience match your target segment?
  • Content alignment — does their tone and content style suit the brand?
  • Credibility signals — do they have a genuine relationship with their audience?
Four KPIs to evaluate before partnering
  • Engagement rate — likes + comments / followers, benchmarked by tier
  • Audience demographics — age, location, gender, via platform analytics or influencer tools
  • Follower quality — ratio of real vs bot or inactive accounts
  • Brand-safe content history — past partnerships, controversial posts, values alignment
The three criteria are a qualitative filter; the four KPIs are a quantitative filter. In practice, good influencer selection uses both. The qualitative criteria come first: if the audience fit is wrong, no engagement rate will compensate. The most common selection error students make in case studies is optimising for reach or follower count without checking audience fit. Emphasise: a micro-influencer with 15,000 followers who speaks directly to New Zealand sole traders is worth more to Hnry than a macro-influencer with 400,000 followers whose audience is broad lifestyle consumers. On follower quality: bot accounts are a significant problem in influencer marketing. Tools like HypeAuditor or Modash can surface the estimated ratio of real vs fake followers. Students should know this check exists even if they haven't run it themselves.
Influencer marketing

Structuring the collaboration

Key elements of a brief
  • Campaign objectives
  • Key messages
  • Deliverables: post type, quantity, timeline
  • Usage rights
  • Exclusivity clauses
Too much control kills authenticity. Too little risks off-brand content. The brief should direct without over-scripting.
Disclosure obligations in New Zealand
The Advertising Standards Authority (ASA) and Commerce Commission both require that paid or gifted partnerships are clearly disclosed. #ad or #sponsored is the standard. Non-disclosure can constitute misleading conduct under the Fair Trading Act.
The brief structure is standard across most campaign types, but influencer briefs have a unique tension that other formats do not: the more you control the creative, the less it sounds like the influencer. Students often assume the brand should write the script — push back on this. The most effective influencer content sounds like the creator, not the brand. The disclosure point is non-negotiable and worth spending time on. In New Zealand, the Fair Trading Act applies to marketing communications, and the ASA has issued specific guidance on influencer disclosure. The standard is clear: if there is a commercial relationship — whether paid, gifted, or otherwise — it must be disclosed. Non-disclosure is not a grey area. Ask students: "Have you ever seen an influencer post that you suspected was sponsored but not labelled? What did that do to your perception of the brand?"
Influencer marketing

Measuring campaign performance

Frame by objective first — KPIs follow from goals, not the other way around.
Awareness campaigns
Reach · Impressions · Follower growth · Share of voice · Earned media value
Engagement campaigns
Likes · Comments · Saves · Shares · Engagement rate
Conversion campaigns
Click-through rate · Website traffic (UTM-tracked) · Promo code redemptions · Cost per acquisition
Key principle: Evaluation KPIs must be set before the campaign launches, not after. You need a baseline to measure against.
The objective-first framing is the most important structural point on this slide. Students often arrive at measurement by listing every metric they can think of. The correct sequence is: define the objective first, then select the KPIs that measure progress toward that objective. An awareness campaign measured by conversion rate is a category error. A conversion campaign measured by impressions is equally misaligned. The UTM tracking point is practical and worth flagging: without UTM parameters on the link in the influencer's bio or post, website traffic from the campaign is invisible in Google Analytics. This is a common gap in influencer campaigns run by small or early-stage businesses. Earned media value (EMV) is a disputed metric — it has value as a rough proxy but should not be reported uncritically. If students use it in their audit work, they should be able to explain how it is calculated and what its limitations are.

Share your reasoning with the group before we move to Section 2.
Give students 4–5 minutes to work through this individually or in pairs, then take 3–4 responses from the group. The intended answer is that nano or micro influencers in the freelancer, contractor, or tradie space are likely to outperform macro influencers for Hnry, primarily because of audience fit and engagement rate. Hnry's value proposition is niche and trust-dependent — it works best when the audience already identifies with the problem Hnry solves. A tradie with 8,000 followers who talks about running their own business is a better fit than a lifestyle creator with 300,000 followers. For KPIs: engagement rate and audience demographics (specifically NZ-based, self-employed or contractor audience) should rank highest. Follower quality is also worth prioritising given the prevalence of bought followers in some niches. Social media audit

Assessing social media presence

Frequency, consistency, and volume of posting
Overview Dashboard
Start here for a high-level view of posting activity. Look for automated insights on audience, activity (posts per week or month), and trends compared to competitors. Bar charts show Hnry's position relative to the landscape average.
Leaderboard
Ranks posting frequency across your competitive landscape. Use the Posts/Month line charts to identify whether Hnry is over- or under-posting relative to MYOB, Xero, and Solo App. This is the cadence calibration view.
Social Posts
Drill into post-level data for timing patterns, frequency trends, and top and bottom performers. Use this to identify whether Hnry's posting schedule is consistent or irregular.
What to record in your audit template: Average posts per week or month across platforms · How Hnry's posting frequency compares to each competitor · Whether the posting pattern is consistent or showing gaps
The Overview Dashboard is the right starting point for students who are new to Rival IQ. It surfaces automated insights that give a useful orientation before diving into the detail. The Leaderboard is where the competitive comparison becomes concrete: students can see at a glance whether Hnry is posting more or less than its competitors and whether that frequency correlates with higher or lower engagement. The key analytical question here is not "how often does Hnry post?" but "is Hnry's posting cadence appropriate given its audience and competitive context?" A brand posting twice a week might be under-investing relative to competitors, or it might be posting at a quality-over-quantity level that serves it well. The data surfaces the pattern; the student supplies the interpretation. Remind students: frequency data without context is not analysis. Connecting frequency to engagement outcomes is where the analytical value is.
Social media audit

Evaluating engagement metrics

Likes, shares, comments, and overall interaction rates
Overview Dashboard
Breaks down engagement by section. Look at dynamic charts for engagement rate, likes, comments, shares, and reactions. These are normalised for fair cross-brand comparison.
Leaderboard
Ranks engagement metrics with competitor averages and time-series line charts. Compare Hnry's likes, shares, and comments relative to audience size against MYOB, Xero, and Solo App.
Social Posts
Reveals per-post engagement breakdowns. Use post tags to categorise high-interaction content and identify what content types are driving Hnry's best-performing posts.
Engagement rate formula: (Total Engagement / Posts) / Followers — the public data calculation Rival IQ uses for fair comparison across accounts of different sizes.
What to record: Hnry's average engagement rate by platform · How it benchmarks against each competitor · Which post types drive the highest engagement for Hnry
Engagement rate is the most important normalising metric in social media analytics. Raw like counts are misleading because they scale with audience size. Engagement rate removes the size effect and enables genuine comparison between accounts. Students often misread high follower counts as a sign of strong performance — push them to look at rate, not volume. The common finding for mid-size accounting and financial services brands is that MYOB and Xero have large audiences but relatively low engagement rates, because their content tends toward product announcements and corporate messaging. Solo App, being smaller and more niche, may show a higher rate despite a smaller reach. If that pattern appears in the data, it is a useful teaching moment: size does not equal effectiveness. When reviewing per-post data, students should look for patterns in content type, not just the top performers in isolation. One viral post can skew averages significantly.
Social media audit

User-generated content and social listening

Mentions, sentiment, and brand conversation
Social Listening
Primary tool for UGC analysis
Monitor mentions, sentiment (via NLP analysis), conversation themes, and user posts across social and web sources including Reddit and Twitter / X. Use the query builder to filter for relevance. Look at emojis, brand praise, and brand criticism separately.
Post Tagging
Isolate UGC and influencer content
In Social Posts, use tags to flag UGC or influencer content. This allows you to isolate and measure content that originated outside the brand's own account.
Discover Tool
Find user creators in Hnry's niche
Use to find user creators in Hnry's niche — self-employment, freelancing, and financial independence content creators in the New Zealand market.
What to assess
Three areas of focus
Volume and sentiment of brand mentions · Whether UGC content is high quality and relevant to Hnry's audience · Whether Hnry is actively engaging with user content or ignoring it
UGC analysis depends on whether Hnry has a meaningful volume of organic brand mentions. If mention volume is low, this is itself a finding about brand conversation and community health.
Social listening is the most often underused feature in Rival IQ for student audits. Many students focus entirely on the brand's own posts and ignore what users are saying about the brand organically. This section asks students to reverse that perspective: what is the brand's community saying, and is the brand listening? For Hnry, the relevant conversation is likely concentrated among sole traders, contractors, and small business operators. The New Zealand freelancer community is active on LinkedIn and, to a lesser extent, Twitter / X. If Social Listening surfaces negative sentiment around fees, tax complexity, or customer service, that is strategically important information for the audit. Conversely, positive UGC from satisfied customers is a potential influencer or ambassador pipeline — connecting this to the influencer marketing framework from Section 1 is a useful synthesis move. Low mention volume is a legitimate finding: it may indicate that Hnry's audience is not a social-sharing community, or that the brand has not yet built enough awareness to generate organic conversation.
Social media audit

Competitor comparison

MYOB, Xero, and Solo App — a side-by-side view
Presence comparison
Overview / Leaderboard
Side-by-side view of posts per week, audience size, and growth trends. Look for patterns: is high posting frequency correlated with higher engagement, or does quality outperform quantity?
Engagement comparison
Leaderboard
Ranks engagement rates, likes, shares, and comments. Identify where Hnry leads, matches, or lags behind each competitor. A competitor posting at high frequency but achieving low engagement signals quality-over-quantity as a strategic implication.
Content strategy comparison
Social Posts
Review the top posts for each competitor. What content formats are driving their best engagement? Identify formats Hnry could adopt and formats Hnry should avoid based on competitor performance data.
What to record: One key strategic observation per competitor · One content format a competitor uses effectively that Hnry does not · One area where Hnry has a measurable advantage
The competitor comparison is the most strategically valuable component of the social media audit. The audit template requires students to go beyond description ("MYOB posts more than Hnry") and offer interpretation ("MYOB's higher posting frequency does not produce proportionally higher engagement, suggesting that content quality or audience relevance is the constraint, not volume"). Push students to make this interpretive move. The three competitor structure is useful because MYOB and Xero represent the established incumbents, while Solo App represents a newer, digitally native challenger with a potentially more engaged niche audience. If Solo App's engagement rate outperforms both incumbents, that is a signal about the strategic value of niche focus and authentic content. Students who are writing up their Assignment 2 audit should use this slide as the backbone for their competitor analysis section, adding specific data points from their Rival IQ landscape.
Activity — Social media audit

Running the Rival IQ analysis

Your task:
Work through the four analysis areas in sequence using Rival IQ. For each area, record your findings directly in your audit template. Use your preferred LLM alongside Rival IQ to help interpret the data — but apply your own critical judgement before accepting any AI-generated interpretation.
The four areas
  1. Social media presence — frequency and consistency across platforms
  2. Engagement metrics — rates and patterns against competitors
  3. UGC and social listening — mention volume, sentiment, and community health
  4. Social media strategy — what is Hnry doing well, and where are the gaps?
Critical review
  • Are the AI findings specific to Hnry's context, or generic social media observations?
  • Does the LLM identify the competitor most similar to Hnry's strategic position?
  • Where does the output require your own judgement to make it useful?
Build a prompt that provides context about Hnry, the competitor landscape, and the specific Rival IQ data. Ask for strategic interpretation and a summary table of findings by area. Share your prompt in the debrief — compare outputs.
Give students 35–40 minutes for this activity. Circulate actively — the most common failure mode is students recording data from Rival IQ without interpreting what it means for Hnry's strategy. A finding like "Hnry posts 3.2 times per week on Instagram" is not useful in an audit without the comparative context (is that above or below the landscape average?) and the strategic implication (should Hnry post more, less, or differently?). For students who are struggling with interpretation, a useful prompt is: "What would you tell Hnry's marketing manager to do differently based on this data?" That forces a practical, action-oriented framing that tends to surface better analysis than purely descriptive approaches. The LLM prompt guidance in the debrief is intentionally minimal — students should construct the prompt themselves rather than following a template. This is a prompt engineering exercise as much as a social media audit exercise. In the debrief, ask two or three students to share their prompts and compare the quality of the outputs they received.

Wrapping up Week 6

  • Influencer marketing works through borrowed credibility — the mechanism explains why authenticity and audience fit matter more than reach alone.
  • Tier selection and KPI setting must be driven by campaign objective, not follower count.
  • Rival IQ makes the social media audit comparative — always interpret Hnry's data against the competitive landscape, not in isolation.
  • AI accelerates the analysis. The interpretation and synthesis are yours.

Connecting the two sections

Close by connecting the two sections explicitly. The influencer marketing framework from Section 1 and the social media audit from Section 2 are not unrelated — a brand's organic social media presence is the context in which any influencer partnership operates. If Hnry's own social content is weak, an influencer campaign may drive traffic to a platform that fails to reinforce the message. Conversely, a brand with strong social content provides better context and credibility for influencer endorsements. The strongest audit reports will make connections like this across sections rather than treating each analysis area in isolation.
