Concept

What is vibecoding?

Using natural language to build functional digital artefacts rather than written content.
Brief section intro. Keep it short and move to the explanation slide quickly.
Concept

Two different things AI can do for you

Content generation
You prompt AI to write something. The output is text: a caption, a brief, an email, a summary. You read it, edit it, and use it somewhere else.
Artefact generation (vibecoding)
You prompt AI to build something. The output is functional: a calculator, a landing page, an analysis tool. You use it directly, share it, or iterate on it.
The skill in both cases is the same. A precise, well-structured brief produces a usable output. A vague brief produces something generic you have to rebuild from scratch.
The word vibecoding comes from describing the experience of building by feel, guided by natural language rather than syntax. Emphasise that this is genuinely accessible to non-technical students and that the outputs can be portfolio-ready.

CRAFT for vibecoding

The same framework, with one element that carries more weight than you might expect.
Brief section intro. The next two slides unpack the framework itself and then focus specifically on Format.
Framework

CRAFT: a prompt engineering framework

  • Context: What is the situation or problem this tool needs to solve? Be specific about the user's circumstances and relevant background.
  • Role: What kind of expert should the AI behave as? A UX designer, a performance analyst, a conversion copywriter?
  • Action: What exactly should the tool do? What inputs does it take, what does it process, what does it return?
  • Format: The structural and design decisions that make the output usable. Sections, fields, layout, visual hierarchy, user journey.
  • Target audience: Who will use this? Their literacy, context, and goals should shape every design decision.
CRAFT was designed for content prompts. It applies equally well to vibecoding, but one element changes meaning significantly when you are building a tool rather than writing copy.
Students may have encountered CRAFT in a content context earlier in the course or in prior study. The framework is the same. The exercise here is to get them thinking about what each element means when the output is functional rather than textual.
Framework

Why Format is different in vibecoding

In a content prompt, Format often means something simple: "three bullet points," "a 200-word paragraph," "a formal tone." In vibecoding, Format is where you make the structural decisions that determine whether the output is actually usable.

Weak Format instruction
"Make it look clean and professional."
Strong Format instruction
"Display results in three sections: a KPI table with benchmark ranges shown as colour indicators, a projected outcomes panel with estimated reach, clicks, and conversions, and a plain-language interpretive note at the bottom explaining what the projections assume."
The gap between those two instructions is the gap between a generic first draft and a tool you could show a client.
Spend two to three minutes on this slide. It is the single most important teaching point in the framing section. Students who write strong Format instructions will produce dramatically better first outputs and spend less time on rework.

Activity 1

KPI and budget calculator

Build a working tool that returns KPIs, benchmarks, and projected outcomes for any nominated marketing tactic and budget.
Transition to Activity 1. Confirm all students have Claude.ai or ChatGPT open before proceeding to the overview slide.
Activity 1

What you are building

A functional app where a user inputs a digital marketing tactic and a monthly budget in NZD, and receives a structured output showing the primary KPIs for that tactic, industry benchmark ranges for each KPI, and projected outcome ranges based on the spend entered.

Input
Tactic name (e.g. Meta Ads, Google Search, SEO, email marketing) and monthly budget in NZD.
Processing
The app identifies relevant KPIs, attaches benchmark ranges, and calculates projected outcomes based on the budget.
Output
Three structured sections: KPI table, projected outcomes panel, and a plain-language interpretive note.
Set expectations for the output before students start. They are not building a dashboard or a research tool. They are building a focused planner that takes two inputs and returns a structured analysis. Scope clarity will save them from over-engineering the prompt.
Activity 1 · Claude.ai or ChatGPT · 25 minutes

Build your KPI calculator

  • Write your prompt (8 min): Use the CRAFT meta prompt to construct your full prompt. Do not open the build tool yet. Write your prompt in a notes document first. Make sure your Format instruction specifies the three output sections clearly.
  • Build and review (12 min): Paste your prompt into Claude.ai or ChatGPT and review the artefact or canvas output. Test at least two different tactics and two different budget levels. Note where outputs feel strong and where they feel uncertain or generic.
  • Reflect (5 min): Whole-group discussion. What worked? Where did Format make the biggest difference? What would you change in a second iteration?
Benchmark reliability varies by tactic. Meta Ads and Google Search have well-documented benchmarks. Influencer marketing and programmatic display are spottier. That variation is worth noting in your interpretive output.
Circulate during Step 2. The most common issue will be students whose Format instruction was too vague and whose output is a wall of text rather than a structured tool. Use those cases as teachable moments in the debrief. Do not prompt students to add an industry field during the build. Let that emerge naturally in the reflection — ask the group what they tried to improve their second iteration and draw out the industry variable as a class observation.
Activity 2

Lovable landing page

Build a professional, hosted landing page for the Hnry Mileage Tracker, targeting New Zealand sole traders and contractors.
Transition to Activity 2. Confirm all students have a Lovable account open at lovable.dev before proceeding.
Activity 2

Your client brief

About Hnry
Hnry is a New Zealand-based financial services platform for sole traders and contractors. It automates tax, invoicing, and expense management so self-employed people can focus on their work rather than their finances.
The Mileage Tracker
A feature within the Hnry app that automatically logs and calculates business mileage for tax purposes. It removes the need for manual record-keeping and ensures sole traders claim every legitimate vehicle expense.
Primary audience: tradespeople, freelancers, and contractors in New Zealand who use a personal vehicle daily for work and file their own tax returns through Hnry.
Give students two minutes to read the brief before they start the meta prompt. They should arrive at the tool with a strategic point of view: what is the single most important pain point? What does the ideal CTA say and why?
Activity 2

Strategic decisions to resolve before you prompt

Your CRAFT prompt for the landing page needs to resolve four questions before you open Lovable.

  • What is the single most important pain point? Manual mileage logging is tedious, error-prone, and causes sole traders to miss legitimate deductions.
  • What sections does the page need? Suggested structure: hero with headline and CTA, pain and solution section, three to four key benefits, social proof, and a final CTA.
  • What is the primary CTA and why? Consider: "Start tracking free," "Connect your Hnry account," or "See how much you could claim."
  • How specific is your target audience in the prompt? Not just "sole traders" — write "tradespeople and contractors in New Zealand who use their vehicle daily for work."
The specificity question is the most important one. Generic prompts produce generic pages. Students who write "New Zealand tradie who drives to job sites every day" will get better copy than students who write "small business owner." Push them to be specific before they start.
Activity 2 · Lovable · 25 minutes

Build your Hnry landing page

  • Write your prompt (8 min): Use the CRAFT meta prompt to construct your landing page prompt. Resolve the four strategic questions on the previous slide before you open Lovable. The Format instruction should specify every section the page needs.
  • Build and review (12 min): Paste your prompt into Lovable at lovable.dev and review the generated page. Does the headline speak to the pain point? Does the CTA reflect a considered choice? Does the page feel like it was written for a New Zealand tradie or does it feel generic? Make one round of targeted refinements.
  • Reflect (5 min): Whole-group discussion. How did the Format element shape the page structure? Where did the tool default to something generic and what did it take to move it toward something specific?
Lovable produces a hosted URL. You can share your landing page right now. That is the standard you are building to.
The reflection question to close this activity is: what is the relationship between brief quality and output quality? Students should be able to articulate that clearly by the end of this activity. It connects back to every other discipline in marketing — from creative briefs to media briefs to research briefs.
Synthesis

What both activities have in common

The brief determines the output
In both activities, the gap between a generic result and a usable one came down to how precisely the prompt specified what was needed.
Format is strategic
Specifying structure is not a technical task. It is a strategic one. Deciding what sections a tool or page needs requires marketing knowledge, not coding knowledge.
AI as a collaborator
The model brings capability. You bring context, judgement, and purpose. Neither produces a good result without the other.
Vibecoding is a strategic communication skill, not a technical one. The same discipline that makes a good AI prompt makes a good creative brief, a good media brief, and a good research brief.

IOA practice

Interactive oral assessment practice

Rehearsing your ability to explain and defend your strategy choices verbally.
Brief section intro. Students have already completed a full practice round last week so this block is rehearsal and confidence-building rather than introduction.
IOA practice · 30 minutes

Practice IOA session

You completed a full practice round last week. Today's session is about building confidence and sharpening your responses under light pressure.

  • Rotate roles as examiner and respondent as per last week's format.
  • Respondents should aim to answer without referring to notes. Use notes only to recover if you lose your thread.
  • Examiners: push for specificity. "Why that tactic?" and "what would you do if that benchmark wasn't achievable?" are more useful than yes/no questions.
  • Allow time at the end of each round for brief peer feedback: one thing that was strong, one thing to sharpen.
The IOA is not a test of memory. It is a test of whether you understand your strategy well enough to defend it in your own words.
Keep the energy practical rather than anxious. Students who have done the preparation will feel the difference between having a point of view and reciting notes. The goal is to surface that difference and give them time to close the gap before the assessment.
