Strategic framework

The strategic hierarchy

Goal
The broad business ambition
A high-level statement of what the organisation is trying to achieve. Not time-bound; not directly measurable. Provides direction.
↓
Objective
A SMART, measurable outcome
Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound. One objective contributes to one goal. The objective is the commitment; it is what gets measured.
↓
Tactic
The specific digital marketing activity
The action taken to achieve the objective. Must be channel-specific, actionable, and measurable. Multiple tactics can support one objective.
Hnry worked example
Goal
Grow Hnry's customer base and product adoption across target markets
Objective
Acquire new UK Hnry customers
Example tactic
Create a direct response video ad campaign targeting UK sole traders across paid social channels
The hierarchy is the structural foundation for the entire session and for Assignment 3. Students frequently conflate objectives and tactics — "increase our social media presence" is not an objective, it is a vague tactic-shaped goal. An objective must be SMART: you must be able to answer "did we achieve it?" with data at the end of the campaign period. Walk through the distinction carefully before moving on. The Hnry example at the bottom uses placeholders that the instructor should complete with the course-defined wording before the session. Do not leave the placeholders blank in a live session.
Strategic framework

KPIs vs KSFs — they are not the same

KPI — Key Performance Indicator
A metric that measures progress toward an objective
Answers: "How are we performing?"
Examples
  • Organic traffic up 20% month-on-month
  • Sign-up conversion rate 3.5%
  • Cost per lead under $25
  • Email open rate 28%
KSF — Key Success Factor
A condition that must be in place for tactics to succeed
Answers: "What has to be true for this to work?"
Examples
  • Landing page load speed under 2 seconds
  • Consistent publishing cadence — at least two posts per week
  • Ad creative reviewed and approved before campaign launch
  • GA4 conversion tracking correctly configured
Both appear in Assignment 3, and they are not interchangeable. A KPI is measured after the fact; a KSF is a precondition that must be in place before the tactic can work.
This distinction trips up many students in Assignment 3. A common error: listing "good content quality" as a KPI, when it is actually a KSF. You cannot measure "good content quality" as a performance indicator — it is a condition you either meet or don't before you launch. Walk through one example of each explicitly: "organic traffic up 20% month-on-month" is a KPI because you can check it with data after the fact. "Consistent publishing cadence of at least two posts per week" is a KSF because if you don't have that in place, your social media tactic cannot work, regardless of how good your content is. Ask students: "Can you spot an example of a KSF being incorrectly listed as a KPI in your Assignment 2 audit?"

Strategic framework

Worked example — objective end to end Example only

Objective: increase sign-up traffic from organic search by 30% within the campaign period
Element Content
Tactic Develop and optimise landing pages targeting high-intent transactional keywords identified in the SEMrush keyword gap analysis
KPI 1 Ranking position for five target keywords — tracked weekly via position tracking tool
KPI 2 Organic traffic to the sign-up page — measured as sessions from organic channel in GA4
KSF Consistent content publishing cadence — minimum two SEO-optimised pages per fortnight throughout the campaign
Measurement tool GA4 (traffic and conversions) · SEMrush Position Tracking (keyword rankings)
Notice how each KPI is directly measurable with a named tool, and the KSF is a precondition — not a metric. This is the structure your Assignment 3 tactics table needs.
Walk through this example carefully before students do Exercise 1. The most important teaching point is the specificity: each KPI names both what is being measured and the tool that measures it. Students who write "track performance" as a measurement approach will not score well. The KSF is correctly framed as a precondition — it is something that must be in place for the tactic to have a chance of working, not something you measure after the fact. Ask students: "If the KSF is not met — if publishing drops below the minimum cadence — what happens to the KPIs?" The answer is obvious once the relationship is clear.
Exercise 1 — 15 minutes

AI-assisted tactic generation

Your task — three steps
  • 1.Choose one of Hnry's two objectives.
  • 2.Construct a CRAFT prompt and use your preferred LLM to generate 3–5 digital marketing tactics that could achieve that objective.
  • 3.For each tactic the LLM suggests: accept as is, modify, or reject. Write one sentence explaining your decision.
Output
A list of 3–5 tactics with a brief justification for each decision you made about the LLM's suggestion. This list is the starting point for your Assignment 3 tactics section.
Debrief question
Which tactics did you modify or reject, and why? What did the LLM miss about Hnry's context?
CRAFT reminder — your prompt must include:
C — Context: who Hnry is, who it serves R — Role: the analytical perspective A — Action: generate digital marketing tactics F — Format: numbered list with rationale T — Target: the specific objective chosen
⚠ Do not use the LLM's output uncritically. Your grade depends on your judgement, not the model's.
Give students 10–12 minutes for the generation and decision step, then 3–4 minutes for debrief. The most important instruction is the accept/modify/reject step — this is what turns a passive AI output into an active analytical decision. Students who copy the LLM's tactics without interrogating them are not doing the exercise. Good LLM prompts for this task will include specific context about Hnry (NZ fintech, sole traders, SaaS sign-up model) and name the specific objective. Weak prompts will say "suggest digital marketing tactics for a fintech company" — the output will be too generic to be useful. In the debrief, listen for students who modified tactics to make them Hnry-specific: that is the analytical move you want to surface and reinforce. Common LLM errors to anticipate: suggesting tactics that are too expensive or complex for the campaign budget, recommending channels where Hnry's audience is not present, or proposing tactics that belong in a long-term strategy rather than a campaign.
KPIs and measurement

The six KPI categories

A framework overview — KPI lists sit under each category, not on this slide.
01
Lead generation
Measuring the volume and quality of new prospects entering the acquisition funnel — sign-ups, trial starts, form completions.
02
Brand awareness
Measuring how widely and clearly the brand is known — reach, impressions, share of voice, branded search volume.
03
Website and traffic
Measuring the volume, source, and quality of website visits — sessions, channel mix, bounce rate, time on site.
04
SEO
Measuring organic search visibility and performance — keyword rankings, organic traffic, backlink profile, domain authority.
05
Paid advertising
Measuring the efficiency and return of paid media — CPC, CTR, conversion rate, ROAS, cost per acquisition.
06
Social media
Measuring presence, engagement, and community — follower growth, engagement rate, reach, share, and brand mentions.
This is a framework overview slide — it should take no more than 3–4 minutes. The purpose is to give students the six-category vocabulary before the next slide asks them to prioritise. Don't list specific KPIs within each category here; the point is to establish the categories as a navigation structure. Ask students as you move through: "Which of these six categories do you think matters most for Hnry's campaign, given what you found in your audit?" Hold the answer for the next slide.
KPIs and measurement

Which categories matter most for Hnry?

Not all six categories deserve equal attention in a short-term campaign. Prioritisation is an analytical decision — your audit should inform it.
Likely primary
Brand awareness & paid advertising
Hnry has an awareness gap, particularly in newer markets. Short-term campaigns can move awareness metrics within the campaign window. Paid media is directly controllable and produces measurable results quickly.
Likely secondary
Website and traffic & lead generation
Traffic and conversion metrics confirm whether awareness activity is producing downstream results. Without these, you cannot tell if your campaign is driving business impact — only that people saw it.
Depends on the objective
Social media
Relevant if the campaign uses social as a primary channel. Secondary if the campaign is primarily paid search or content. What your audit found about Hnry's social presence should shape your decision.
SEO metrics are not campaign KPIs. Meaningful ranking improvements take three to six months. Do not include SEO as a primary KPI in a short-term campaign — see Slide 15 for detail on where SEO findings belong.
This slide is designed to provoke critical thinking, not to hand students the answer. The framing ("likely primary", "likely secondary", "depends on the objective") signals that these are analytical judgements, not rules. The key teaching point is that SEO is explicitly excluded from campaign KPIs — this is addressed in more detail in Slide 15, but it is worth flagging early so students don't build their KPI frameworks around SEO metrics. The warn callout is important: it is a common error in Assignment 3. Ask students: "Based on your own audit, which one of these categories do you think Hnry is currently underperforming in, relative to competitors?" This primes them for the audit-to-strategy exercises later.
KPIs and measurement

Benchmarks — why they matter for Assignment 3

What a benchmark is
An industry-standard or channel-standard reference point that tells you what "good" performance looks like for your tactic and channel. Benchmarks give your predictions credibility.
The principle
A prediction without a benchmark is a guess.
If you predict a 4% conversion rate, you need to be able to explain why 4% is a reasonable expectation. Without a benchmark, your prediction has no analytical foundation.
How to find benchmarks
Search for industry-specific benchmarks for your chosen tactics and channels. These are widely published by major advertising platforms, email marketing providers, and marketing research firms. Be specific: a SaaS conversion rate benchmark is more useful than a generic website benchmark.
In Assignment 3: Every predicted KPI needs a benchmark source. State the benchmark, its source, and why it applies to Hnry's context. Benchmark sources should be cited in your reference list.
Benchmarks are required in Assignment 3 but students frequently omit them or use them vaguely ("industry average"). The teaching point here is specificity: a SaaS free-trial sign-up conversion rate benchmark for a B2C product targeting sole traders in the NZ market is a very different number from a general e-commerce conversion rate. Students should be looking for benchmarks that are as specific to Hnry's context as possible, and being transparent when they are forced to use a less specific proxy. Where to find benchmarks: major advertising platforms publish quarterly or annual benchmark reports for paid search and paid social. Email marketing platforms publish email benchmark reports. Marketing research firms publish sector-specific reports. Students should search for these directly rather than relying on a single tool or slide-presented list.

Connecting your audit

The bridge from audit to strategy

The audit identified
What is and isn't working in Hnry's digital presence — measured, evidenced, and benchmarked.
The strategy is
The plan to address those findings — targeted, resourced, and measurable within the campaign period.
They must reference each other directly.

A proposal that ignores the audit is a missed opportunity — and a marking gap. The strongest proposals show a direct line from an audit finding to a strategic decision.
The question to ask yourself
Which two or three findings from your audit are most strategically significant — and what do they imply you should prioritise?
This slide is conceptual and should take about 3 minutes. The key message is simple but important: the audit and the proposal are not two separate assignments — they are the diagnostic and the prescription for the same patient. The marking rubric for Assignment 3 will expect students to explicitly reference their audit findings when justifying strategic choices. Ask students: "Think of two findings from your own audit. What do each of those findings imply about what Hnry should do differently?" Hold their answers for the worked example on the next slide.
Connecting your audit

Worked example: audit finding → tactic → KPI Example only

Step Content
Audit finding SEMrush keyword gap analysis showed that Hnry has limited visibility for transactional search terms (e.g. "sole trader tax NZ", "invoice tool for freelancers") that competitors rank for on page one.
Strategic implication Hnry is missing high-intent acquisition traffic from users who are actively looking for a solution — not just brand-aware visitors. Addressing this gap should increase sign-up conversion quality.
Tactic Develop and optimise three to five landing pages targeting the highest-priority transactional keywords from the keyword gap analysis, each with a clear CTA to the free trial sign-up.
KPIs Ranking position for five target keywords (SEMrush Position Tracking) · Conversion rate on those pages (GA4 goal completions)
KSF Pages must be technically sound — load time under 2 seconds, mobile-optimised, no crawl errors surfaced in Site Audit
The finding → implication → tactic sequence is the analytical chain Assignment 3 is looking for. Each step must follow logically from the one before.
This worked example models the full analytical chain students need to produce in Assignment 3. Walk through it step by step: the finding is specific and evidenced (from a named tool); the strategic implication is an interpretation, not a restatement of the data; the tactic is specific and actionable; the KPIs name both the metric and the measurement tool; the KSF is a genuine precondition. Ask students: "Can you identify a specific finding from your own audit that could follow this same chain?" If students can answer this question, they are ready for Exercise 3. Note: the example uses an SEO finding but maps it to a campaign tactic (landing page development), not an SEO metric. This is an important distinction — see Slide 15.
Connecting your audit

How each audit section feeds into your campaign

Some audit findings lead to campaign tactics. Others lead to strategic recommendations that sit outside the campaign itself.
Audit section What it informs in your proposal
Website audit Campaign tactics — conversion rate and UX improvements have fast, measurable impact within the campaign window. Landing page quality directly affects paid and organic conversion performance.
Paid advertising audit Campaign tactics — channel selection, ad creative decisions, offer types, and budget allocation all flow directly from what the audit found about current paid performance and gaps.
Social media audit Campaign tactics — content strategy, community activity, and organic social can all be executed and measured within the campaign window. Competitor gaps inform what Hnry should test.
SEO audit Strategic recommendations — NOT campaign tactics. See callout below.
SEO findings belong in your strategic recommendations, not your campaign tactics. Meaningful ranking improvements take three to six months — your campaign budget should focus on activities that show measurable results within the campaign period. If your audit surfaced SEO issues, flag them as longer-term priorities in your proposal's strategic recommendations section.
This is one of the most practically important slides in the deck for Assignment 3. The SEO callout addresses the most common structural error students make: putting SEO as a primary campaign tactic with a KPI like "rank on page one for three keywords within six weeks." This is unrealistic and analytically unsound. SEO timelines are measured in months, not weeks. The correct treatment is to acknowledge SEO gaps from the audit, include them in strategic recommendations, and focus the campaign budget on tactics that produce measurable results within the campaign period. Walk through the table row by row. The worked example on Slide 14 showed how an SEO audit finding (keyword gap) can inform a campaign tactic (landing page development) without making ranking improvement itself the KPI. That distinction is the nuance to reinforce here.
Exercise 3 — 10 minutes

Connecting your audit to your strategy

Your task — three steps
  • 1.Open your Assignment 2 audit.
  • 2.Identify two or three specific findings — not general themes, but specific data points or observations from the audit data.
  • 3.For each finding, note: what the finding was, what it implies strategically, and which tactic in your emerging campaign it supports. If the finding relates to SEO, note it as a strategic recommendation, not a campaign tactic.
Output — integration map
Finding Implication Tactic / Rec
Finding 1……
Finding 2……
Finding 3……
This map can be used as justification evidence in your Assignment 3 proposal.
Share one row from your integration map. Can the group trace the logic from finding to tactic? If not, the implication needs more work.
Give students 7–8 minutes. The most important instruction is "specific data points or observations" — not general themes. "Hnry has low SEO performance" is a theme. "SEMrush shows Hnry ranks outside the top 50 for 'sole trader tax NZ' while MYOB holds position three" is a specific finding. The specificity matters because vague findings produce vague strategies. Circulate and push students toward specificity if their findings are too general. The debrief is designed as a peer check: if other students cannot trace the logic from finding to tactic, the implication is under-developed. This is useful formative feedback before Assignment 3. Students who identify SEO findings should be explicitly directed to put them in the strategic recommendations column, not the tactic column — reinforcing the Slide 15 point.
Campaign structure

What a complete campaign structure looks like

The full architecture you need for Assignment 3 — follow this map when writing your proposal.
Goal
→
Broad business ambition — the "why" of the campaign
Objectives
→
Two SMART objectives, each contributing to one goal
Tactics
→
3–5 specific digital marketing activities per objective, each channel-specific
KPIs & KSFs
→
Two KPIs and one KSF per tactic, with measurement tool named for each KPI
Benchmarks
→
Industry benchmarks for primary KPIs — sourced and cited, used to justify predictions
Budget
→
Allocated by tactic, with rationale. Each tactic has a predicted return based on benchmarks
Predicted outcome
→
What the campaign will achieve for each objective — evidenced by benchmarks, not guessed
This is the structural map for Assignment 3. Give students time to read each row carefully — this is what they are building. The flow from Goal to Predicted outcome is a chain: every element must be traceable back to the goal. A budget allocation with no link to a tactic, or a predicted outcome with no benchmark to justify it, will lose marks. Ask students: "At which step in this chain do you feel least prepared right now?" The answers will tell you which parts of the session to spend more time on or revisit in the following week.
Campaign structure

Budget framing

Principle 1
Budget follows objectives
Allocate based on what each objective requires, not arbitrary splits. A high-priority objective should receive a proportionally higher budget.
Principle 2
Every tactic needs a predicted return
Use benchmarks to estimate what your spend should produce. If you cannot estimate a return, you cannot justify the allocation.
Principle 3
Justify, don't guess
A budget with no rationale will lose marks. Every allocation should reference a benchmark, a cost estimate, or a comparable campaign outcome.
Example budget table structure — use placeholder figures, replace with your own:
Tactic Channel Budget allocation Predicted metric Benchmark source
Paid social awareness campaign Meta (Facebook & Instagram) $X per month X impressions at $X CPM [Industry benchmark report]
Search retargeting Google Ads $X per month X clicks at $X CPC [Platform benchmark data]
Content and landing pages Organic / owned $X (production cost) X organic sessions within campaign window [SEMrush / GA4 baseline]
Budget framing is often the weakest section of student proposals. The three principles on this slide address the three most common errors: (1) arbitrary splits without justification, (2) allocations with no predicted return, and (3) guesses presented as analysis. The example table is intentionally incomplete — students should build their own version with specific figures from their tactic decisions. The benchmark source column is critical: it forces students to attribute their predicted metrics to a real reference point. Walk through the example table and ask students: "What would go in the benchmark source column for a Meta awareness campaign? Where would you find that data?" This prompts them to think about where they need to do research before finalising their proposal.

Before next week

Your Assignment 3 preparation checklist

  • Draft a campaign structure with at least two tactics per objective — each one channel-specific and actionable.
  • KPIs and KSFs identified for each tactic. Check: are your KSFs preconditions, or are they outcomes you have mislabelled?
  • Measurement approach specified for each KPI — named tool, not just "track performance".
  • At least two audit findings mapped to tactics in your strategy. SEO findings should be in your strategic recommendations, not your campaign tactics.
  • Industry benchmarks sourced for your primary KPIs — at least two citations ready for your reference list.
Office hours
Bring specific questions — not general ones. "How do I find benchmarks for Meta ads in the SaaS sector?" will get a useful answer. "How do I do Assignment 3?" will not.
The audit gave you the evidence. You now have the framework to turn it into a strategy. The quality of Assignment 3 depends on how well those two things talk to each other.
Close the session by reviewing the checklist with students. Ask for a show of hands: how many have at least started on item 1? Item 4? This surfaces where the cohort is and what support they need before the Assignment 3 deadline. The office hours framing is deliberate: specific questions produce specific help. Students who arrive at office hours with vague anxiety about the assignment will benefit more if they have first attempted a draft and can point to the specific part they are stuck on. Close with the italicised framing in the right panel: the audit and the proposal are one continuous analytical project, not two separate tasks.
