Welcome to Week 4 tutorial. Before starting, confirm that all students have logged into their SEMrush accounts — they should have received access via the course LMS. Students who haven't activated their account should do so now before Step 1. This tutorial is hands-on: students should complete each step on their own device as you walk through the slides. Expect Step 3 (Site Audit) to take some time to process in the background — launch it early and move forward while it runs. The full setup (Steps 1–9) is designed to be completed in a 50-minute tutorial. Steps 1–4 are highest priority; Steps 5–9 can be completed independently if time runs short. Bridge to content: "SEMrush is the primary tool you'll use for Assignments 2 and 3. Getting it set up correctly today means every subsequent tutorial builds on real Hnry data."
1
Step 1
Create your Hnry project in SEMrush

You already have a SEMrush account. The next step is to create a dedicated Project for Hnry so that all your analysis tools — Site Audit, Position Tracking, and more — live in one place.

Click Projects in the left menu, then Create new project. Enter hnry.co.nz as the domain and name the project "Hnry NZ". This container will hold every tool you configure across the semester.

Why this matters: A project keeps your Hnry analysis organised and separate from any other domains you explore.
Common issues at this step: (1) Students entering "www.hnry.co.nz" — advise them to use the bare domain "hnry.co.nz" without the www prefix. SEMrush will detect the canonical version. (2) Students who cannot find the Projects tab — it is in the left sidebar, sometimes collapsed. Ask them to look for a folder icon. (3) Free-tier accounts may have a project limit — if students hit a limit, they should delete any test projects created during onboarding. Once everyone has a project created, confirm the dashboard is showing the Hnry project before moving to Step 2. The project name "Hnry NZ" is a convention — students can use any name, but consistency will help when you refer to the project in later tutorials.
2
Step 2
Get your baseline SEO footprint

Before configuring any tools, open Domain Overview from the main SEMrush interface and enter hnry.co.nz. Set the country database to New Zealand. Review the organic traffic estimates, number of ranking keywords, top countries driving traffic, and main competitors listed.

Click into Top Organic Keywords → View details to see the full keyword list and current rankings. Take a screenshot — this is your baseline before any deeper configuration.

Why this matters: The Domain Overview gives you a snapshot of where Hnry currently stands in search before you start auditing.
Walk students through the Domain Overview metrics. Key things to highlight: Organic Traffic is an estimate, not actual GA data — explain that SEMrush models traffic from click-through rates applied to keyword rankings. Authority Score (0–100) is a composite metric combining backlink profile, organic traffic, and spam signals — it is similar to Moz's Domain Authority. Ranking Keywords: ask students to note the number and compare with competitors they identify. The "New Zealand" database is important — hnry.co.nz targets NZ primarily, so national data is more relevant than global. Ask students: "What keywords do you expect Hnry to rank for?" Before they look at the data — this primes them to think strategically rather than just record whatever SEMrush shows. Screenshot prompt: "Take a screenshot of the Domain Overview now. You'll need this for Assignment 2 as your baseline data point."
3
Step 3
Set up your first Site Audit

In your Hnry project dashboard, click Site Audit and create a new campaign for hnry.co.nz. In General Settings, set the crawl limit to 5,000 pages and choose Website as your crawl source. Set the crawl frequency to weekly.

Launch the audit and wait for it to finish — this typically takes between 5 and 30 minutes depending on site size. Once complete, review the overall health score, errors, warnings, and thematic reports to identify technical and on-page issues.

Why this matters: The Site Audit reveals technical problems that may be holding back Hnry's organic visibility — this is the foundation of any SEO analysis.
Launch the audit at the start of this step and move forward — it runs in the background. Students can return to review results during Steps 5–7. Key settings to watch: crawl limit of 5,000 is more than sufficient for hnry.co.nz (the site has fewer than 500 indexed pages based on current estimates). Crawl source: "Website" is correct; "Sitemap" is an alternative but requires knowing the sitemap URL. Weekly frequency is appropriate for a monitoring setup. When the audit completes, direct students to the three headline metrics: Health Score (%), Errors (red), and Warnings (yellow). Common errors to anticipate on hnry.co.nz: missing alt text on images, slow page load times, and potentially some duplicate meta descriptions across product pages. Remind students: "The Site Audit is a diagnostic, not a verdict. Every site has issues. What matters is whether the issues are significant and whether Hnry is addressing them."
4
Step 4
Track where Hnry ranks for key terms

In the project dashboard, click Position Tracking. Enter hnry.co.nz, then configure: search engine as Google, device as both desktop and mobile, and location as New Zealand. Add an initial keyword set by importing from the Domain Overview results you reviewed in Step 2.

Add 2–3 competitor domains to benchmark against — Hnry's main NZ competitors are a useful starting point. Save and start tracking. Rankings will populate within 24 hours.

Why this matters: Position Tracking turns a one-off keyword snapshot into an ongoing view of Hnry's visibility, so you can measure the impact of any strategy you recommend.
For competitor domains, suggest students add: myob.com (large incumbent), xero.com (dominant NZ accounting SaaS), and possibly freeagent.com (UK-origin competitor with NZ presence). These three represent the main competitive set Hnry faces in NZ search. Keyword import from Domain Overview: students should import the top 20–30 keywords by traffic. They can refine the list after completing the Keyword Magic Tool step. Mobile vs Desktop tracking: explain that Google uses mobile-first indexing, so mobile rankings are now the primary signal — but desktop often differs. Running both gives the full picture. Rankings take 24 hours: this is a good point to emphasise that SEO is not instant. Ask students: "Why do you think tracking competitors' rankings alongside Hnry's might be useful?" Expected answer: to understand relative movement — if Hnry improves but competitors improve faster, the net position may not have changed.
5
Step 5
Research and expand Hnry's keyword universe

From the left menu, open Keyword Overview and enter seed keywords relevant to Hnry — for example "self-employed tax NZ", "freelancer tax New Zealand", and "sole trader accounting". Review volume, keyword difficulty, and CPC to identify which terms are worth targeting.

Use the Keyword Magic Tool to expand these seeds into related terms, long-tail phrases, and questions. Focus on high-intent, realistic targets: moderate difficulty, meaningful volume, and directly relevant to Hnry's services. Export or save these lists — they will feed into your Position Tracking and content planning later in the course.

Why this matters: A strong keyword list is the foundation of both the SEO audit (Assignment 2) and the campaign proposal (Assignment 3).
Keyword Difficulty (KD%): explain that KD is scored 0–100. For a relatively small site like hnry.co.nz, realistic targets are KD 0–40. Terms above 60 are likely dominated by major publications or large-authority competitors (MYOB, Xero, IRD). Volume in NZ context: NZ has a small population (~5 million), so monthly search volumes will look low — 50–200/month for a specific term is meaningful in NZ. Students often dismiss low-volume terms, but in a small market these can be highly valuable. CPC: useful as a proxy for commercial intent. Terms with high CPC are terms advertisers are bidding on, which suggests strong buyer intent. Seed keywords to walk through live: "sole trader tax NZ", "invoice app NZ", "hnry tax" (branded). Have students identify at least 5 non-branded target keywords before moving on. Bridge: "These keywords will go into your Position Tracking campaign (Step 4) and will also structure the keyword section of your Assignment 2 audit."

Step 6
Identify page-level improvements

In your Hnry project, open On Page SEO Checker. Add Hnry's key landing pages (start with the homepage and top product or service pages) and map your target keywords to each page.

Run the tool to receive suggestions on content improvements, semantic keyword usage, and on-page optimisation. Cross-reference these recommendations with the Site Audit issues for the same URLs — for example, a page flagged for thin content in the audit may also be under-optimised according to the On Page Checker.

Why this matters: This is where diagnostics become specific, actionable recommendations — exactly what your audit report needs to demonstrate.
Key landing pages to add: hnry.co.nz (homepage), hnry.co.nz/features (or equivalent product page), hnry.co.nz/pricing, and hnry.co.nz/blog (if a blog exists). Map keywords from Step 5 to the most relevant page — for example, "sole trader accounting NZ" maps to the homepage or features page. The On Page Checker generates three types of recommendations: Strategy (backlink opportunities and semantic ideas), Ideas (content improvements for the specific page), and Technical (on-page technical issues). Focus on the Ideas tab first — this is where actionable content suggestions live. Cross-referencing with Site Audit: if Site Audit flagged the homepage for "low word count" and On Page Checker recommends adding semantic keywords, these are the same problem viewed from two angles. Assignment 2 note: "When you write up your audit report, you should cite both tools where they corroborate each other — this shows triangulated analysis, not just a list of tool outputs."
7
Step 7
Understand Hnry's link profile

From the main menu, open Backlink Analytics and enter hnry.co.nz. Review total backlinks, referring domains, and authority metrics. Then open Backlink Audit within your project to identify any toxic or low-quality links.

Look at the backlink profiles of Hnry's competitors to spot potential outreach or digital PR opportunities — sites linking to competitors but not to Hnry represent a gap worth noting in your analysis.

Why this matters: Off-page authority affects how well Hnry ranks; understanding its backlink health helps you assess the full picture of its SEO performance.
Key metrics to highlight in Backlink Analytics: Referring Domains (more important than total backlinks — 100 links from 100 different domains is better than 1,000 links from one domain), Authority Score of referring domains (higher = more valuable), and follow vs. nofollow ratio. Toxic score: SEMrush assigns a toxicity score (0–100) to backlinks. Scores above 45 are flagged as potentially harmful. For hnry.co.nz, there are likely very few toxic links — it is a legitimate, actively marketed product. Backlink Gap vs. competitors: this is the most strategically useful output for Assignment 3. A site linking to MYOB and Xero but not Hnry is a potential digital PR target. Ask students: "What types of sites do you think would link to a freelancer tax tool?" Expected answers: freelancer community sites, NZ business publications (Stuff Business, BusinessDesk), accounting and finance blogs, IRD adjacent resources.
8
Step 8
Benchmark Hnry against the competition

In Domain Overview or Organic Research, enter 2–3 of Hnry's main NZ competitors and compare their organic traffic, keyword counts, and top-performing pages. Then use the Keyword Gap tool to identify keywords competitors rank for that Hnry does not — these are your opportunity areas.

Add these competitors to your Position Tracking campaign so you can monitor relative movement over time. Document the gaps you find — they should directly inform the strategic recommendations in your audit report.

Why this matters: Your analysis of Hnry only becomes meaningful when you understand how it stacks up against the alternatives its customers might choose instead.
Recommended competitors to use in the Keyword Gap analysis: xero.com, myob.com, and either freeagent.com or hnry.co.uk (Hnry's own UK site, to compare how the same brand performs in a different market). Keyword Gap interpretation: the tool returns four categories — Missing (Hnry has no ranking, competitors do), Weak (Hnry ranks but lower than competitors), Strong (Hnry outperforms), and Untapped (none rank, but all have it in their keyword universe). Focus on Missing and Weak — these are direct strategic gaps. Top-performing pages: comparing competitor pages that drive the most traffic reveals content format and topic preferences that Hnry may not be addressing. For example, if Xero has a "sole trader tax guide NZ" that drives significant traffic, this is both a keyword gap and a content gap. Assignment 2 framework: "The competitive gap section of your audit should explain why each gap exists and what Hnry would need to do to close it — not just list that the gap exists."
9
Step 9
Turn SEMrush into an ongoing system

In the project dashboard, configure email alerts or scheduled PDF reports for Site Audit health score, errors, and Position Tracking changes for key keywords. Set a review cadence: weekly for technical health and rankings, monthly for keyword and content strategy.

Use the SEMrush SEO Dashboard as your at-a-glance view — it surfaces visibility trends, traffic estimates, keyword movement, and open issues in one place. This transforms SEMrush from a setup exercise into a live monitoring tool you can reference throughout the semester.

Why this matters: Ongoing monitoring means you can track whether Hnry's SEO health is improving or deteriorating — and demonstrate that awareness in your proposal defence.
Email reports: set these up to a personal email address. Students are not required to submit reports as evidence, but receiving weekly updates keeps them aware of SEMrush data passively. Scheduled reports can be PDF or CSV — PDF is more accessible for non-technical stakeholders and is the format most likely to be used in a professional context. Position Tracking alerts: configure alerts for significant ranking changes (e.g., movement of ±5 positions for key terms). This mirrors professional practice where SEO managers set up alerts for major fluctuations. SEO Dashboard: walk students through the dashboard widgets — Visibility Score, Traffic trend, ranking distribution (top 3 / 4–10 / 11–20 / 21–50 / 51–100), and open Site Audit issues. Proposal Defence connection: "Examiners will ask you to demonstrate active use of SEMrush. A student who can show trend data over several weeks — and explain what changed and why — demonstrates a higher level of engagement than a student who ran a one-off audit."
