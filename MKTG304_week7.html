Section 1 — Google Display Network

Google Display Network

Reach, targeting, bidding, and campaign design for visual advertising across the web.
We will start with the Display Network, then move to Search PPC in the second half. The GDN is often underestimated as a channel — students tend to think of it as banner ads. The reality is it is a sophisticated, AI-powered system for reaching audiences across millions of placements. Let's start with the fundamentals.
Google Display Network

The Google Display Network

What it is
  • Millions of websites, apps, and video platforms
  • Reaches 90%+ of internet users globally
  • Ads appear while users browse — not while they search
  • Formats: image, responsive display, video, rich media
Demand Gen — the extended reach
Demand Gen campaigns extend display-style ads across YouTube, Discover, and Gmail. Part of Google's "Power Pack" of AI-driven campaign types alongside Performance Max and AI Max.
Key distinction from Search: Display is passive — your ad appears while someone reads an article or uses an app, not because they searched for your product. This makes it powerful for brand awareness and remarketing.
The GDN is Google's network for placing visual ads across the web. The key distinction from search is that display is passive — your ad appears while someone reads an article or uses an app, not because they searched for your product. This makes it powerful for brand awareness and remarketing. The formats are visual: banner images, responsive ads that adapt to different placements, video, and interactive rich media. One development worth knowing is Demand Gen, which is Google's newer campaign type that pushes display-style creative across YouTube, Discover feeds, and Gmail. It sits alongside Performance Max and AI Max in what Google calls the "Power Pack." Think of Demand Gen as GDN's reach extended into Google's owned surfaces.
Google Display Network

GDN bidding: CPC vs CPM

CPC — Cost Per Click CPM — Cost Per Mille
You pay for Each click Every 1,000 impressions
Best for Conversions and traffic Brand awareness and reach
Control Performance-focused Visibility-focused
Hnry scenario Australia: drive sign-ups in a market with existing awareness UK: build recognition where brand awareness is near zero
Match your bidding strategy to your campaign objective. The choice flows directly from what you are trying to achieve — not from what is cheapest.
Two main bidding options on the GDN. CPC means you only pay when someone clicks — ideal when you want measurable actions like sign-ups or purchases. CPM means you pay per thousand impressions regardless of clicks — ideal when the goal is getting your brand in front of as many relevant people as possible. The choice flows directly from your objective. If Hnry wants awareness in the UK where nobody knows them, CPM makes sense. If they want sign-ups in Australia where they already have traction, CPC is the better fit. We will work through both scenarios in the exercises.

Targeting on the GDN falls into two broad categories: audience-based and contextual. Audience targeting is about who you want to reach. Demographics are straightforward. Affinity audiences capture long-term interests — someone who is passionate about small business, for example. In-market audiences are people actively researching a purchase or solution right now. Custom segments let you define your own audience using keywords people have searched, URLs they have visited, or apps they use. And remarketing lets you re-engage people who have already been to your site. Contextual targeting is about where you want your ad to appear — on pages about certain topics, containing certain keywords, or on specific websites you choose. Under advanced options, optimised targeting is important to understand. This is Google's AI-driven expansion tool. You give it your conversion data and first-party audiences as signals, and it finds additional users likely to convert, even outside your manually selected segments. This replaced Similar Audiences, which Google removed entirely in 2023. The shift reflects Google's broader move toward AI-driven targeting powered by first-party data rather than third-party cookie-based lookalikes.
Practice #1 — GDN brand awareness

GDN brand awareness for Hnry UK

Scenario
Hnry has launched in the UK: 1% brand awareness, 4 million self-employed workers. Test budget: £500 GBP (~$1,000 NZD). Goal: build recognition among creative freelancers and independent contractors.
Your questions
  1. Which targeting options best reach UK sole traders for an awareness goal?
  2. What bidding strategy — and why?
  3. Estimate impressions for the budget (research a realistic UK CPM)
  4. What KPIs measure awareness success when conversions are not the goal?
Hint
Research typical CPM ranges for financial services display advertising in the UK market to estimate impressions from the budget.
Cold-start awareness scenario. Hnry has virtually no brand recognition in the UK — targeting and bidding logic is fundamentally different from a market with existing traction.
This is a cold-start awareness scenario. Hnry has virtually no brand recognition in the UK, so the challenge is fundamentally different from selling to people who already know you. Walk students through the targeting thinking: affinity audiences around freelancing, self-employment, and small business ownership would capture long-term interest. In-market audiences for accounting software or tax services would reach people actively looking. Custom segments built from keywords like "freelancer tax UK" or "sole trader accountant" and URLs of competitor sites like FreeAgent or Crunch would be highly relevant. For bidding, CPM makes sense here because the goal is visibility, not clicks. Have them research average CPMs for UK financial services display — typically £3 to £7. At £5 CPM, £500 buys roughly 100,000 impressions. Success metrics for awareness include impressions, reach, frequency, and view-through conversions rather than direct click-based conversions.
Practice #2a — GDN conversion campaign

GDN conversion campaign for Hnry Australia

Scenario
Hnry Australia: 23% brand awareness, 1.5m self-employed Australians. Test budget: $1,000 AUD. Goal: drive sign-ups.
Work through this:
  1. Which targeting options best support a conversion goal?
  2. What bidding strategy?
  3. How many clicks does the budget yield?
  4. How many sign-ups can Hnry expect?
Now we shift from awareness to conversions. Australia is a more mature market for Hnry — people have heard of them, product-market fit is proven, and the goal is to get sign-ups. Targeting shifts accordingly: remarketing to previous website visitors is high priority, in-market audiences for accounting and tax software, and custom segments built around competitor URLs and relevant search terms. Bidding should be CPC or Target CPA since the goal is measurable actions. The maths: $1,000 divided by $0.75 CPC gives approximately 1,333 clicks. At a 2.8% conversion rate, that is roughly 37 sign-ups. Have students work through this themselves before revealing the answer.
Practice #2b — Calculating ROAS

ROAS for a subscription product

ROAS = Revenue from advertising ÷ Cost of advertising
The SaaS twist
Hnry earns recurring revenue. Average customer generates approx. $1,200 AUD/year and stays ~2.5 years.
Customer Lifetime Value (CLV) = $3,000 AUD
Your calculations (using Practice #2a results)
  1. Calculate first-year ROAS
  2. Calculate CLV-based ROAS
  3. Does the campaign meet the 4:1 benchmark on either measure?
The 4:1 benchmark is designed for e-commerce with one-time purchases. For SaaS, the relevant question is whether CPA is below a fraction of CLV — not whether ROAS clears 4:1 on year one alone.
This is where it gets interesting. A one-off product like a phone has a simple ROAS calculation — revenue from the sale divided by ad cost. But Hnry is a subscription business. Each customer pays roughly $1,200 per year and stays for about 2.5 years on average, giving a CLV of $3,000. Working through the maths from Practice #2a: 37 sign-ups multiplied by $1,200 first-year revenue equals $44,400, divided by the $1,000 spend gives a first-year ROAS of roughly 44:1. Using CLV it is even higher: 37 multiplied by $3,000 equals $111,000, so 111:1. These are illustrative numbers, but the point is powerful — subscription businesses can afford much higher CPAs than single-purchase businesses because each customer generates revenue over years. The 4:1 benchmark that people cite as a minimum is really designed for e-commerce with one-time purchases. For SaaS, the more relevant question is whether the CPA is below a fraction of the CLV. This is an important strategic insight for their A3 proposals.

Section 2 — Google Search

Google Search PPC vs SEO

PPC SEO
Speed Hours / days Months / years
Cost model Pay per click Investment in content
Control High — targeting, budget, copy Limited — algorithm-dependent
Visibility Guaranteed (bid-dependent); now in AI Overviews Organic; AI Overviews may reduce CTR
Best for Immediate traffic, testing Long-term authority
Use both. PPC for now, SEO for later.
Quick comparison before we move into Search PPC exercises. The fundamental trade-off is speed versus sustainability. PPC gives you immediate visibility but costs per click. SEO takes months to build but delivers sustainable organic traffic. Most effective strategies use both — PPC for immediate results and testing, SEO for compounding long-term value. Two developments to note. First, Google now shows ads within AI Overviews — those AI-generated summaries at the top of search results. This is a new surface for PPC. Second, AI Overviews can reduce organic click-through rates for some informational queries, which changes the SEO calculus. Worth keeping both in mind as you think about channel strategy. For your A3 proposals, Hnry's Objective #1 brief excludes search channels, so you would not propose Google Search PPC or SEO. But Display, Demand Gen, YouTube, and other non-search Google Ads products are all fair game. Understanding how search works is still essential context for any digital marketer.

The direction of travel: AI Max
AI Max
Launched 2025. Broader keyword matching, dynamic URLs, automated creative assembly from your headline and description assets.
The trend: Advertisers provide strategic signals. AI handles execution. The quality of your inputs matters more than ever.
Bidding on Search PPC ranges from fully manual to fully automated. Manual CPC gives you complete control — you decide what each keyword is worth. Enhanced CPC was the bridge between manual and automated, adjusting your bids to chase conversions, but Google is deprecating it in favour of fully automated options. On the automated side, Maximise Clicks is the simplest — spend the budget getting the most clicks possible. Target CPA and Target ROAS are smarter because they optimise toward business outcomes, not just traffic. Maximise Conversions is similar but without a specific cost target. The bigger picture here is AI Max, which launched in 2025. It represents Google's vision for the future of search advertising — less reliance on manual keyword lists, broader matching powered by AI, and automated creative assembly from your headline and description assets. The trend is clear: advertisers provide the strategic inputs and the AI handles execution. That does not make the human role less important — it makes the quality of your inputs more important. Bad conversion tracking, weak creative assets, and unclear business objectives get amplified by AI just as much as good ones do.
Practice #3 — Search PPC for Hnry NZ

Search PPC for Hnry NZ

57% brand awareness · 400,000 self-employed New Zealanders · Test budget: $1,000 NZD · Average CPC (NZ fintech): $1.40 · Conversion rate: 3.2% · Avg. revenue: $1,000 NZD/year
Keyword strategy
  1. Give three keyword examples across different search intents:
    Informational · Commercial · Transactional
  2. Should Hnry bid on competitor terms?
    What are the risks and benefits?
Budget planning
  1. Clicks: $1,000 ÷ $1.40 = ?
  2. Sign-ups: clicks × 3.2% = ?
  3. First-year ROAS: (sign-ups × $1,000) ÷ $1,000 = ?
This exercise brings together keyword strategy and budget maths. For keywords, push students beyond generic terms. Informational intent might be "how to pay tax as a freelancer NZ" — someone researching, not ready to buy. Commercial intent: "best tax software for sole traders NZ" — comparing options. Transactional: "Hnry sign up" or "automatic tax payment freelancer" — ready to act. On competitor bidding, it is a genuine strategic question. Bidding on "Xero sole trader" or "FreeAgent NZ" can capture people comparing options, but it is expensive (competitors bid up the price) and the landing page needs to address the comparison directly or quality score suffers. For the maths: $1,000 divided by $1.40 CPC gives about 714 clicks. At 3.2% conversion, that is roughly 23 sign-ups. Revenue: 23 multiplied by $1,000 equals $23,000, giving a first-year ROAS of 23:1. Strong, but remind students these are illustrative figures.
Mockup exercise — Responsive Search Ad

Create a Responsive Search Ad for Hnry

How RSAs work
Up to 15 headlines (30 chars each) + 4 descriptions (90 chars each). Google's AI assembles the best combination per query. You provide the pool — Google tests and optimises.
Your mockup
  • At least 5 headlines
  • At least 2 descriptions
  • Sitelinks, call asset, structured snippets
Tool: karooya.com/responsive-search-ad-preview-tool
Key decisions
Which headlines would you pin?
Pin your brand name to position 1. Pin your core value prop to position 2. Let Google rotate the rest.
Which would you let Google rotate?
Feature variations, social proof, seasonal or audience-specific CTAs.
RSAs replaced Expanded Text Ads in 2022. The shift from fixed copy to AI-assembled combinations is the same philosophical move as AI Max — you provide assets, AI optimises delivery.
This is the current default ad format for Google Search. Unlike the old Expanded Text Ads, which showed exactly what you wrote, RSAs give Google a pool of headlines and descriptions to mix and match. You provide up to 15 headlines and 4 descriptions, and the AI tests combinations to find what works best for each search query. The tool at Karooya lets you preview what different combinations look like. For the Hnry mockup, strong headlines might include the brand name, the core value proposition ("Tax paid as you earn"), a trust signal ("NZ's #1 for sole traders"), a feature ("Automatic tax calculations"), and a CTA ("Start your free trial"). Pinning means locking a headline to a specific position — pin your brand name to headline 1 so it always appears, but let Google rotate the others. Descriptions should expand on benefits: mention that Hnry also files your returns, handles ACC, and provides an accountant. Ad assets (formerly called extensions) add sitelinks to pages like pricing, how it works, and reviews. These increase your ad's real estate on the results page and improve click-through rates.
Google Ads in 2026

Google Ads in 2026: What's changing

AI is the engine
Power Pack — AI Max + Performance Max + Demand Gen. These three campaign types work together to drive campaign delivery. Advertisers provide signals; AI handles execution.
Ads in AI Overviews
Paid results now appear inside AI-generated search summaries at the top of results. A fundamentally new surface for PPC — changing where and how ads are seen.
Legacy formats retiring
ETAs gone (2022). Call-only ads: new campaigns blocked Feb 2026, serving ends 2026–27. Direction: flexible, asset-based formats that AI assembles.
First-party data is critical
Similar Audiences removed (2023). Third-party cookies phasing out. Targeting now relies on your own customer data and conversion signals as inputs to Google's AI.
Hnry’s Objective #1 excludes search, but Display, Demand Gen, and YouTube are all valid for your A3 proposals.
Four things shaping Google Ads right now. First, AI is no longer an add-on — it is the core of how campaigns run. Google's Power Pack gives you three AI-driven campaign types that work together: Demand Gen for awareness, AI Max for search intent, Performance Max for full-funnel scale. Second, ads now appear inside AI Overviews. When Google generates an AI summary at the top of results, your ads can show within it. This is a fundamentally new surface. Third, Google is systematically retiring legacy ad formats. Expanded Text Ads went in 2022. Call-only ads cannot be created from February 2026 and will stop serving by 2027. The direction is toward flexible, asset-based formats that AI assembles. Fourth, first-party data matters more than ever. With Similar Audiences gone and third-party cookies being phased out, your own customer data and conversion signals are what power Google's automated targeting. For A3 proposals: Hnry's Objective #1 excludes search channels, so Google Search PPC is off the table — but Display, Demand Gen, and YouTube are all valid and encouraged. Think about what signals and assets you would provide to Google's AI for those campaign types.
