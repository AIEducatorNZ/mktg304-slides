Course Learning Outcomes

LO1
Examine the fundamentals of digital marketing and its impact on organisational performance.
LO2
Audit a live case's existing digital marketing practices to identify areas where marketing efforts are most needed and provide recommendations for improvement.
LO3
Complete an industry-recognised certification specific to digital marketing by applying knowledge and skills gained through the course.
LO4
Outline a data-driven digital marketing campaign for a given live case that aligns with an organisation's overall marketing goals and addresses target-market needs.
LO5
Select and justify digital marketing analytics tools and key metrics to determine success across websites, social media, email and other digital channels.
These are course-level outcomes: the full semester moves from understanding, to auditing, to campaign development, to professional justification.
Clarify that these are the official course-level learning outcomes, not outcomes students are expected to achieve during Week 1. Briefly connect them to the course journey: students first develop foundational knowledge, then audit the client's current digital presence, complete industry certifications, develop a data-driven campaign, and justify their decisions and evaluate performance. Emphasise the progression from understanding, to analysis, to recommendation, to professional justification.
Course Delivery

Course Delivery and Expectations

Effective course delivery depends on three pathways to engaged learning.
Experiential
Learn by applying ideas to a real organisation.
Collaborative
Learn with your team and through class discussion.
Attendance
Be present for live activities, feedback and demonstrations.
Effective Course Delivery
Explain that effective delivery depends on participation from both the teaching team and the students. Experiential learning means students will learn by applying concepts to a real organisation rather than working only with hypothetical examples. Collaborative learning means students will learn from their team members, classmates and the discussion taking place during class. Attendance matters because much of the value comes from live activities, demonstrations, feedback and interaction that cannot be fully reproduced by reading slides later. The purpose is not attendance for its own sake. Attendance enables the other two pathways.

Why AI Matters for You — Right Now

The world has changed
  • AI is flooding the world with cheap, fast knowledge.
  • What used to be scarce — information, facts and formulas — is now free and everywhere.
  • Employers are already adjusting: fewer jobs require a degree, and wage premiums for knowledge alone are falling.
So what does that mean for students?
Success isn't about knowing more than AI.
It's about doing what AI can't: thinking critically, making ethical decisions, collaborating and being creative.
Stress that this is not an argument that knowledge no longer matters. Students still need sufficient knowledge to judge whether information and AI outputs are correct. The shift is that access to information is no longer the primary source of advantage. The advantage increasingly comes from how effectively someone can interpret, challenge, combine and apply that information. Connect this directly to the course: students will be expected to make decisions and justify them, not simply reproduce definitions or generate polished-looking content.

What Universities Must Do, Part 1

1
The old model
Pay tuition, get access to, and acquire knowledge.
2
The new reality
Everyone has access to information and knowledge. The value now lies in how you apply it: judgement, originality and adaptability.
3
What this means for you
Don't just memorise. Challenge, question, build and create.
Your edge is not what you know, but what you do with what you know.
Explain that the old university value proposition depended partly on privileged access to experts, information and credentials. Generative AI weakens the scarcity of information, but it does not eliminate the need for universities. It changes what universities must help students develop. Make clear that knowledge remains foundational. The point is that knowledge by itself is less differentiating when everybody can generate an immediate answer. The course will therefore place substantial emphasis on applying ideas to ambiguous, real-world problems.

Introduce CREATER as a set of capabilities that become more valuable, not less valuable, as AI improves. Do not read every description word for word. Briefly illustrate the larger message: Critical thinking is needed to evaluate AI output. Accountability matters because responsibility cannot simply be transferred to a tool. Collaboration and emotional intelligence remain fundamentally human. Reflection and adaptability matter because the tools and expectations will continue changing. Explain that these skills will be practised through the client work and interactive assessments rather than taught only as abstract concepts.
Global Evidence on Gender Gaps & Generative AI
22%
Women are approximately less likely than men to use generative AI tools, even after controls.
42% / 31%
Women account for 42% of ChatGPT users and 31% of Claude users.
Explain that AI adoption is not distributed evenly. The gap should not be framed as a lack of ability. The research identifies several possible contributors, including differences in familiarity, confidence, access to training, ethical concerns and fear of being judged for using AI. Connect this to the purpose of the course. All students need structured opportunities to develop competence and confidence with these tools. Ask students to consider what might happen if one group gains substantially more experience using productivity-enhancing technology than another group.

Why AI Matters in New Zealand — and to You

AI Is Transforming the Economy

  • $611M+ invested in AI-related R&D projects in New Zealand.
  • $76B in potential GDP uplift by 2038.
  • Potential uplift equivalent to more than 15% of GDP.
  • By 2030, global labour-market transformation is projected to create 170M jobs, displace 92M, and produce a net increase of 78M.

Business Adoption

  • 67% of large New Zealand enterprises now use some form of AI.
  • Up from 48% in 2023.
  • 68% of SMEs have no plans to evaluate or invest in AI.
  • Lack of expertise is a major barrier to adoption.

Students and Generative AI

  • UK undergraduate use of AI rose from 66% to 92% in one year.
  • Only 36% have received institutional support to develop AI skills.
  • The broad lesson for New Zealand: student adoption is rapid; formal skills support must keep pace.
Use this slide to move from the broader argument to students' immediate context. The New Zealand figures show both considerable economic opportunity and an adoption gap. Larger organisations are moving rapidly, while many smaller organisations remain uncertain about how to begin. The skills shortage is therefore especially relevant. Employers do not simply need people who have tried an AI chatbot; they need people who can use AI productively, critically and responsibly within a business context. For the student figures, be precise: the HEPI research is based on UK undergraduates. Do not present it as New Zealand survey data. Use it to illustrate the speed of adoption and the support gap likely to concern universities internationally. Clarify that the World Economic Forum jobs figures are not all "AI jobs." They reflect broad labour-market transformation driven by several forces, including technological change, the green transition, demographic shifts and economic pressures. The key message is that jobs and required skills will change substantially, making adaptability and AI fluency increasingly important.

Course engagement is worth the same as the industry-certification component.
Spend time clarifying A1 because students may underestimate the engagement component. The industry certifications together account for 7.5%, while course engagement independently accounts for another 7.5%. Engagement therefore carries the same total weight as the certification component. Explain the overall assessment journey: A1 builds professional skills and rewards active engagement. A2 diagnoses the client's current digital position. A3 develops a campaign response. A4 allows each student to explain and defend their understanding. For A3, make the distinction between the group campaign component and the individual AI reflection clear. Students share responsibility for the campaign proposal but are individually accountable for reflecting on their AI use. Avoid overwhelming students with detailed instructions today. The complete briefs and rubrics will be introduced separately.
