Engagement Questions

Week 3  ·  Lecture  ·  2026
Opening engagement questions to warm up the room before the main content. Use these to prime students for the buyer persona and journey activities, and to surface prior knowledge of content marketing and AI search. Suggested questions: (1) Have you ever built a buyer persona before — for a class project, at work, or on your own? (2) In the last week, how many times did you use an AI tool like ChatGPT or Perplexity to search for something instead of Google? (3) If you were hiring a freelance graphic designer in New Zealand, what tool would you use first to research your options? Take hands and use the responses to frame the session — especially question 3, which sets up the GEO section later.
Section 1 — Prompt Engineering Activity

Building a Buyer Persona

Hnry | Prompt Engineering Activity
Frame this section as a hands-on prompt engineering activity. Students will apply what they learned about few-shot prompting and the CRAFT framework to a real business context: Hnry. Remind students to have their AI tool of choice open (ChatGPT, Claude, or Gemini) before the activity slides. This section covers two personas: one for the NZ market focused on mileage app adoption, and one for the UK market focused on attracting new users.

Activity Objective

We are building two buyer personas for Hnry
Persona A
Market: New Zealand

Focus: Mileage app adoption

Driving uptake of the Hnry mileage tracking feature among sole traders who currently manage their own records manually.
Persona B
Market: United Kingdom

Focus: Attracting new users

Acquiring new Hnry users in a market where brand awareness is lower than in New Zealand.
Context: Hnry is a Wellington-based fintech that automates tax, invoicing, and financial admin for sole traders and freelancers — operating in NZ, Australia, and the UK.
Briefly introduce Hnry for students who may not be familiar: Wellington-based fintech founded in 2017, now operating across three markets. Core product automates the financial administration that freelancers and sole traders typically have to manage themselves — tax calculations, GST filing, invoicing, and mileage tracking. The mileage feature is a relatively recent addition and a key NZ-specific growth lever. The UK market is newer and faces a different competitive landscape (Coconut, Crunch, FreeAgent). Ask: "Who has heard of Hnry?" — this calibrates how much context you need to give before the activity.

How We Will Work Through This

  • We will build one persona at a time — working through each step together before moving to the next.
  • Step 1: Build the New Zealand persona — mileage app adoption.
  • Step 2: Build the United Kingdom persona — attracting new users.
  • Before we start: find your few-shot reference (next slide).
Your prompt will include three inputs: the Hnry market context, your few-shot reference from the next slide, and a clear task instruction. Structure your prompt using the CRAFT framework — Context, Role, Action, Format, Target Audience.
This slide sets the pace. Running both personas sequentially (rather than simultaneously) is intentional — it allows students to reflect on what the NZ persona revealed before building the UK one, and makes the comparison more meaningful. Remind students that they are practicing the few-shot technique specifically: they are providing the model with an example to guide the format and depth of the output. The CRAFT framework reference is a callback to last week's content — use it to reinforce the framework as a practical tool, not just a theoretical construct.

Finding Your Few-Shot Reference

Before running the persona activity, find 2–3 buyer persona examples or templates online.
What a template gives you
A template identifies the fields your persona should include — name, demographics, goals, frustrations, channels, preferred content format, decision-making process. It shows the model the shape of the output.
What a completed example adds
A filled-in example adds depth and tone — it shows how specific the language should be, how much detail to include for each field, and what level of insight is expected.
Why this is few-shot prompting
Giving the model a pattern to follow before your task instruction is few-shot prompting. It dramatically improves format consistency and output quality compared to asking without an example.
Tip: HubSpot, Semrush, and Xtensio all publish good buyer persona templates. Search "buyer persona template" and pick one that resonates with how you think about audiences.
Give students 2–3 minutes to find a template or example before moving on. This pause is deliberate — it makes the few-shot technique tangible rather than theoretical. Good search terms: "buyer persona template marketing", "HubSpot buyer persona", "B2B buyer persona example". Circulate and check what students are finding. If some are finding very generic templates, encourage them to look for examples closer to B2C fintech or subscription software. The specificity of the example shapes the specificity of the output. Bridge to the next slide: "Now that you have your reference, let's build Persona A."

Section 2 — Prompt Engineering Activity

Building a Buyer Journey

Hnry | Prompt Engineering Activity
Transition from persona to journey map. The key framing: a buyer journey map takes a persona and traces their path through the decision process — from first awareness of a problem through to becoming a loyal customer. It adds a temporal and experiential dimension that the persona alone does not capture. The same prompt engineering principles apply: few-shot examples improve format quality, CRAFT structures the context, and specific context produces specific output. The activities in this section build directly on the persona outputs from Section 1.

Activity Objective

We are building two buyer journey maps — one for each persona
Journey A
Persona: NZ sole trader

Focus: Mileage app adoption

Trace the journey from first awareness of the mileage tracking problem through to adoption of the Hnry feature.
Journey B
Persona: UK freelancer

Focus: Attracting new users

Trace the journey from first awareness of a financial admin problem through to becoming a Hnry user.
Output: A buyer journey map that covers awareness, consideration, decision, and post-purchase stages — with touchpoints, emotions, and barriers at each stage.
A buyer journey map extends the persona by adding: (1) stages — typically awareness, consideration, decision, retention; (2) touchpoints — where the buyer encounters the brand or information at each stage; (3) emotions — how the buyer feels at each stage; (4) barriers — what stops them from moving forward. Good journey maps are specific to a persona, not generic. This is where the persona outputs from Section 1 become the most important context input — the journey should feel like it belongs to that specific person. If a student's persona output was weak, their journey map will be generic. Use this as a teaching moment about the compounding effect of context quality across a multi-step prompt chain.

How We Will Work Through This

  • We will build one journey at a time, following the same sequence as the persona activity.
  • Step 1: NZ buyer journey — using the NZ persona from Section 1 as context.
  • Step 2: UK buyer journey — using the UK persona from Section 1 as context.
Key principle: Each journey map output should feel like it belongs to the specific persona you built — not a generic template with the persona's name applied. Specificity in your context block drives specificity in the output.
Prompt chaining in action: This activity is an example of prompt chaining — the output of the persona prompt becomes a key input for the journey map prompt. Each step validates before proceeding: if your persona feels weak, refine it before moving to the journey map.
The prompt chaining framing is an explicit callback to Week 2's advanced techniques section. This activity demonstrates exactly what prompt chaining looks like in a marketing workflow: Step 1 (audience research → persona) feeds into Step 2 (persona → journey map). Students who rushed through Section 1 will now encounter the downstream consequence of a weak persona. This is a valuable real-world lesson: in practice, every step of a content or strategy process is only as good as the inputs it receives. Take a moment to ask: "Did anyone refine their persona after building it? What did you change?" This surfaces the iterative principle from Week 2's Principle 5.

Finding Your Few-Shot Reference

Find 2–3 buyer journey map examples or templates online before starting the activity.
What a template gives you
A template identifies the stages and fields your journey map should include — stages, touchpoints, emotions, barriers, opportunities. It shows the model the structure of the output.
What a completed example adds
A filled-in example adds depth and specificity — it shows how granular touchpoints should be, what kinds of emotions are captured, and how barriers are framed.
Why format matters
Journey maps can be structured as tables, timelines, or stage-by-stage narratives. Pick the format that best suits how you will use the output — and specify it explicitly in your Format block.
Tip: Miro, HubSpot, and Nielsen Norman Group all publish good buyer journey map examples. Search "customer journey map template B2C" or "buyer journey map example fintech".
Again give students 2–3 minutes to find a template. Buyer journey maps vary more in format than buyer personas — some are stage-based tables, some are timeline diagrams, some are emotion-curve charts. The format students choose will shape what the model produces. Good formats for this activity are tabular (stages as columns, rows for touchpoints, emotions, and barriers) because they are easy to prompt for and easy to compare across the two personas. Bridge: "Now let's look at what goes into your prompt before running the activity."

Context for Your Prompt

Three inputs to include in your prompt before running the activity:
  • Your completed buyer persona from Section 1
    Paste the full persona output — or a structured summary of its key fields. This is the most important context input. The journey map should feel like it belongs to this specific person.
  • The Hnry market research report
    Include relevant market context — touchpoints the persona would actually encounter, competitive alternatives they might consider, and the specific Hnry feature or outcome driving the journey.
  • Your few-shot buyer journey example or template from Slide 11
    This provides the format pattern. Include it before your task instruction — either pasted in or described precisely (e.g., "use a table with columns: Stage, Touchpoints, Emotions, Barriers, Opportunities").
CRAFT check: Context (Hnry + persona + market) → Role (e.g., senior CX strategist) → Action (build the journey map) → Format (table or narrative) → Target Audience (who will use this output — your team, a client, an exam examiner).
This slide is the prompt design scaffold — it tells students exactly what to include before they run the activity. The CRAFT check at the bottom is a direct callback to Week 2's framework. Walk through it explicitly: "Your context block should include three things: the persona, the market report, and the format reference. Your role might be something like 'senior CX strategist with B2C fintech experience'. Your action is specific: build a buyer journey map for [persona name]. Your format specifies the table structure. And your target audience tells the model who this is for — which shapes the vocabulary and assumed knowledge." This slide is worth spending 2–3 minutes on before releasing students to the activity.

The content strategy implication: Hnry's UK content should invest more heavily in awareness and education content than its NZ content, where existing brand recognition means consideration-stage content is more valuable. This is a direct application of buyer journey analysis to content strategy — a preview of the GEO section that follows.

This section is a mini lecture — shift mode from activity facilitation to direct instruction. The statistics set the stakes immediately: these are not niche tools. ChatGPT has more weekly active users than most social networks had when content marketers first had to take them seriously. What is GEO?

"Generative Engine Optimisation (GEO) is the practice of structuring content and digital presence so that AI-powered platforms — including ChatGPT, Google AI Overviews, Perplexity, Gemini, and Copilot — retrieve, cite, and recommend a brand when generating answers to user queries."
— Content Marketing Report, MKTG 304, 2026
How it fits within content marketing
Content marketing has always been about earning attention through valuable content. GEO extends this to a new distribution surface: AI-generated answers. The goal is no longer just to rank on page one of Google — it is to be cited inside AI responses when a user asks a relevant question.

If traditional SEO was about earning a position among ten blue links, GEO is about earning a place among the two to seven sources a large language model cites in a single generated response.
Why it matters now
Gartner predicts up to 25% of searches will migrate to generative engines by 2028.

Google AI Overviews already appear in at least 16% of all searches — with much higher prevalence for comparison and research queries.

One documented case (Tally): ChatGPT became the primary referral source for a bootstrapped B2B tool in 2025. LLM referrals grew 800% year-on-year for Backlinko over a three-month window.
Source: Content Marketing Report, MKTG 304, Section 4.1. The term GEO was formally coined by Aggarwal et al. at ACM SIGKDD 2024 — the first peer-reviewed academic framework for the discipline. The competitive environment of GEO is more concentrated than SEO: a page-one Google result might be among ten; an AI citation might be among two or three. But the reward is qualitatively different: when an AI system names a brand in its answer, it confers an implicit endorsement that a blue link never could. The Tally case is particularly compelling for students because it illustrates GEO's impact at scale for a small company — not just a large brand with an existing SEO advantage. Ask: "If you were searching for the best accounting tool for freelancers in New Zealand, and ChatGPT gave you a recommendation, how would you treat that recommendation compared to a Google ad?" This frames the trust dimension of AI citation.

Academic Foundations

Aggarwal et al. (2024) — "GEO: Generative Engine Optimization" — ACM SIGKDD Conference on Knowledge Discovery and Data Mining. The first peer-reviewed framework for GEO.
What the research did
Tested 7 distinct GEO strategies on a benchmark dataset of 10,000 diverse queries, with Perplexity.ai as the primary generative engine. Measured which content modifications most improved citation frequency in AI-generated responses.
Most effective strategies
Statistics Addition — adding specific numerical data
Quotation Addition — attributed expert quotes
Fluency Optimisation — improving clarity and coherence
What did NOT work
Keyword stuffing performed worse than the baseline in GEO experiments. This reinforces the qualitatively different nature of the two optimisation challenges — tactics that retain some SEO value can actively harm GEO performance.
"GEO establishes that visibility in generative engine responses is learnable, measurable, and improvable through specific content modifications."
Aggarwal et al., 2024
Source: Content Marketing Report, MKTG 304, Section 4.3. The significance of the Aggarwal et al. study is that it moves GEO from intuition to empirical science. Before this paper, practitioners were guessing at what content modifications would improve AI citation rates. The study provides the first systematic, reproducible evidence. The keyword stuffing finding is particularly important because it signals that GEO and SEO are not just different emphases on the same levers — they can pull in opposite directions. A subsequent 2025 paper on citation bias (cited in Search Engine Land) found that AI engines strongly favour earned media over brand-owned content, suggesting that third-party coverage is more important than on-site optimisation for GEO purposes. This aligns with the SEO intuition that backlinks signal credibility, but the mechanism is different: AI systems synthesise from across the web, not just index-rank pages.

GEO vs SEO

Dimension Traditional SEO Generative Engine Optimisation (GEO)
Primary objective Rank on search results pages Earn citations in AI-generated answers
Success metric Rankings, clicks, sessions Citation frequency, share of AI voice, AI-referred traffic
Content structure Keyword relevance, heading hierarchy, meta tags Completeness, clarity, citability, direct answers
Authority signals Backlinks, domain authority score Earned media, third-party mentions, entity recognition
Key tools Google Search Console, Ahrefs, SEMrush Passionfruit Labs, Geoptie, GA4 attribution
Update frequency Quarterly content audits at minimum Ongoing; AI models update training frequently
What it rewards Technical optimisation + keyword relevance Depth, credibility, clarity, and genuine usefulness
Key insight: LLMs are not ranking documents — they are synthesising information to answer questions. Content must teach the model about a brand's expertise and position it as the most credible available source.
Source: Content Marketing Report, MKTG 304, Section 4.2, Table 1. The table is reproduced directly from the report. The most important row for discussion is "Authority signals": in traditional SEO, authority is measured primarily through backlinks. In GEO, it comes from earned media — independent third-party coverage in industry publications, analyst reports, and high-authority platforms like Reddit and LinkedIn. A brand with excellent backlink profile but little earned media coverage may rank well in Google but be invisible to AI systems. Conversely, a brand that has been extensively covered in specialist media may have strong GEO visibility even without a strong SEO presence. The "Key insight" callout is the most important sentence on this slide — spend 30 seconds on it. The difference between ranking and being cited is the difference between being listed and being endorsed.

Practical GEO Strategy

Content
Structure for citability
Write content that answers questions directly. Use clear definitional opening statements, specific supporting data, and attributed expert quotes. FAQ sections and structured comparison content are particularly GEO-effective — they map directly onto the question-and-answer structure of AI queries.

Source: Firebrand Digital GEO Guide, Dec 2025; Aggarwal et al., 2024
Authority
Build off-site presence
AI engines privilege earned media over brand-owned content. Coverage in industry publications, analyst reports, Reddit, LinkedIn, and YouTube generates the independent corroboration that AI systems interpret as credibility signals.

Reddit, LinkedIn, and YouTube were among the top cited sources across leading LLMs in October 2025 (Search Engine Land).

Source: Search Engine Land, 2025; Content Marketing Report §4.4
Technical
Entity and schema optimisation
AI systems use entity recognition to understand who a brand is and how it relates to other concepts. Ensure your brand is accurately represented in Wikipedia, Wikidata, and Google's Knowledge Graph.

Implement Schema.org markup (Organisation, Product, FAQ schema) — this gives AI engines explicit structured signals about your content.

Allow AI crawler access in robots.txt; consider LLM.txt files (emerging 2025 standard).

Source: Content Marketing Report §4.4
Source: Content Marketing Report, MKTG 304, Section 4.4. The three-card structure maps to the three main pillars of GEO strategy: content quality and structure, off-site authority, and technical infrastructure. For most content marketers, the first two pillars are most immediately actionable. The third (technical) requires developer involvement for schema markup and crawler access configuration. The LLM.txt standard (modelled on robots.txt) is emerging but not yet widely implemented — students who work in digital marketing roles in the next few years will likely encounter it. The off-site authority point is the most counterintuitive for students familiar with traditional SEO thinking: you cannot GEO-optimise purely through your own website. You must earn your way into AI responses through the quality and reach of your wider content ecosystem. This connects back to the buyer journey exercise: the touchpoints your persona encounters are the same channels where your GEO presence needs to be strongest.

Measuring GEO Performance

Measurement is the most underdeveloped area of GEO practice in 2025–26. Most marketing teams lack visibility into how often — or how accurately — AI systems represent their brand.
Search Engine Land GEO Guide, 2026
01
AI Citation Frequency
How often the brand appears in AI-generated answers across relevant queries
02
Share of AI Voice
Brand citation rate vs. competitors across a defined query set
03
Citation Sentiment
Whether AI systems represent the brand accurately and positively
04
AI-Referred Traffic
Visits and conversions attributable to AI search — tracked via GA4
Measurement tools (emerging)
Passionfruit Labs and Geoptie — purpose-built GEO measurement platforms that query AI engines directly and monitor brand citation performance over time.

GA4 — track visits and conversions from AI search referrers (ChatGPT.com, Perplexity.ai, etc.) as a revenue-connected outcome measure.
The measurement approach
Treat GEO measurement as an iterative challenge: establish baseline citations across key query sets → track changes as content and authority investments are made → triangulate AI-referred traffic as a revenue-connected outcome.

Methodology standardisation is still developing — expect this landscape to evolve significantly through 2026–27.
Source: Content Marketing Report, MKTG 304, Section 4.5. The measurement challenge is real and not yet solved — this is an honest framing, not a gap to apologise for. Passionfruit Labs and Geoptie are nascent tools; methodology is still developing. The practical recommendation for any organisation starting a GEO programme today is to: (1) manually query key AI tools (ChatGPT, Perplexity, Gemini) with the queries your target audience would use and record whether your brand is cited; (2) set up GA4 to track referral traffic from AI platforms as a source; (3) document this baseline before making any content or authority investments, so you have something to compare against. The HubSpot 2026 State of Marketing found that measuring marketing ROI is the number one challenge for marketers (33% of respondents) — GEO measurement is the newest and hardest version of this perennial problem. Close this section by connecting measurement back to the buyer journey maps students built earlier: the queries your personas would use to research their problem are the same queries you should be monitoring for AI citation performance.
