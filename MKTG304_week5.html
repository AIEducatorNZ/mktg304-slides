Section 1 — 25 minutes

SEO analysis

On-page, technical, and off-page SEO — plus a competitor comparison. Working directly in SEMrush with your AI tool alongside.
Transition into Section 1. Before advancing, confirm that all students have their SEMrush Hnry project open (set up in Week 4). Students who don't have this set up should spend the first few minutes of this section getting it configured — direct them to the Week 4 slides if needed. Remind students to have their audit template open on the side. This section has four slides: what the template requires, verifying the SEMrush setup, identifying which reports to download, and analysing those reports.

Have your audit template open alongside SEMrush throughout this section. Fill in findings as you go, not after.
Walk students through the four areas in the template before they open SEMrush. The key framing: these four areas are not equally weighted in terms of SEMrush tool coverage. On-page and technical SEO are heavily supported by the Site Audit and On Page SEO Checker. Off-page is covered by Backlink Analytics and Domain Overview. Competitor analysis draws on Keyword Gap and Organic Research. Remind students: the template is asking for analysis, not a data dump. Listing that Hnry has X backlinks is not analysis. Explaining what that backlink profile reveals about authority relative to competitors is analysis.

Ask: "Does everyone have at least five of these seven sections showing data?" before advancing. Students who are missing critical sections will have a gap in their SEO analysis that they'll need to note explicitly in their audit report.
Activity — SEO

Using an LLM to identify which reports to download

Your task
Use your preferred LLM to determine which specific SEMrush reports to download for each of the four SEO areas in the audit template: on-page SEO, technical SEO, off-page SEO, and competitor analysis.
Then do this
Download the identified reports from SEMrush. Note which reports the LLM recommended and whether its guidance matched what you already knew from the Week 4 setup.
Prompt guidance:
Craft your own prompt. Include context about what the audit template requires for each SEO area and ask the LLM to recommend specific SEMrush reports or exports that would supply that data. Be specific about the tool (SEMrush) and the site (hnry.co.nz).
Share your prompt with the group. What framing produced the most specific and useful recommendations? What would you refine?
Give students 5–6 minutes. The learning goal here is twofold: (1) practising prompt construction for a specific analytical task, and (2) building awareness of which SEMrush exports are actually useful versus which ones generate noise. Good prompts will specify the four audit template areas explicitly and ask for named SEMrush report types. Weak prompts will ask vaguely about "SEO reports" and get generic answers. In the debrief, listen for whether students noticed the LLM over-recommending paid SEMrush features (Enterprise reports) that may not be available on their plan. This is a good example of where critical evaluation of AI output matters: the LLM doesn't know their account tier. Reports most useful for the audit: Site Audit (Technical), Organic Research export (On-page keywords), On Page SEO Checker suggestions (On-page), Backlink Analytics referring domains export (Off-page), Domain Overview screenshot or export, Keyword Gap export filtered to Missing/Weak terms (Competitor).
Activity — SEO

Analysing the reports with an LLM

Your task:
With your SEMrush reports downloaded, build a prompt that instructs your LLM to analyse the data against the four SEO areas in the audit template. Upload or paste the relevant data, run the analysis, and review the output.
What to include in your prompt
  • Context: what Hnry does and who it serves
  • The four SEO areas from the audit template
  • The reports you are uploading
  • Instruction: analyse and identify key findings for each area
Critical review checklist
  • Are the findings specific to Hnry or generic?
  • Does the LLM misread any data values?
  • What has it missed that you can see in the data?
  • What interpretation requires your own judgement?
Where did the AI interpretation hold up? Where did it need to be challenged or corrected? Note these moments — they are relevant to your audit methodology discussion.
Give students 7–8 minutes. The most common failure mode: students upload the data and accept the AI output without reading it critically. Circulate and look for students who are just copying the output directly into their audit template. Prompt them: "What does this finding actually mean for Hnry's visibility? Is that a significant problem or a minor one?" The critical review checklist on the slide is meant to prompt active interrogation of the output, not a passive read. The methodology note at the bottom is deliberate: Assignment 2 requires students to reflect on their use of AI tools, so pausing to notice where the AI was wrong or incomplete is directly assessable content. Common AI errors to anticipate: misinterpreting percentage changes, conflating Domain Rating (Ahrefs) with Authority Score (SEMrush), and over-stating the severity of technical issues that are minor for a small site.
Section 2 — 15 minutes

Website overview

Evaluating hnry.co.nz across nine audit criteria, combining AI-assisted analysis with mandatory manual review.
Transition into Section 2. This section is shorter (15 minutes) but has a mandatory manual component that cannot be skipped. The key framing: no automated tool, including Manus, will identify ethical considerations. Students need to visit the live site themselves. Before the activity, check that students know what the MetaPrompt is and have used it before. If this is new to some students, take a minute to explain: the MetaPrompt is a tool that generates a well-structured CRAFT-based prompt for a specific task, which is then run in Manus.

Ethical considerations
Criterion 9 cannot be assessed by any automated tool. Cookie transparency, data collection disclosure, and accessibility equity require you to visit the live site and evaluate it manually. This criterion is on the next slide.
Walk through the nine criteria briefly. Most students will be familiar with these from the audit template, but it is worth naming them explicitly before the Manus activity so students know what they are checking against. The key point about criterion 9: Manus will not identify whether Hnry's cookie consent banner is meaningful, whether data collection is disclosed in a way that is easy to understand, or whether the site's accessibility provision is equitable in practice. These require a human visitor with ethical awareness. The red highlight on criterion 9 is intentional and worth drawing attention to. Ask: "Has anyone visited hnry.co.nz and looked at the cookie banner recently?" This primes them for the manual review slide.
Activity — Website overview

Using the MetaPrompt with Manus

  • Generate your prompt using the MetaPrompt
    Use the MetaPrompt to generate a CRAFT-based prompt designed to run a complete website audit of hnry.co.nz against the nine criteria in your audit template. Be explicit in your MetaPrompt input: name all nine criteria and specify that the output should address each one.
  • Run the generated prompt in Manus
    Open Manus at manus.im/app. Paste your generated prompt and run it. Manus will browse hnry.co.nz directly and produce its analysis.
  • Review the output critically against each criterion
    Work through the nine criteria in your audit template. Note where Manus has provided useful, specific evidence and where its output is superficial, generic, or missing. The gaps are as important as the findings.
What did Manus do well? What did it miss or get wrong? Where did you need to apply your own judgement to make the output useful?
Give students 8–10 minutes for this activity. The MetaPrompt step is important: students who skip it and write a weak prompt directly into Manus will get a weaker output. The CRAFT structure helps the generated prompt be specific about what Manus should look for on the site. Common issues: (1) Manus sometimes has trouble with JavaScript-heavy pages and may miss interactive elements — students should note this. (2) Manus will not evaluate cookie consent meaningfully — it may describe that a cookie banner exists but won't evaluate its transparency or compliance quality. (3) Manus may produce surface-level UI observations ("the homepage has a clear CTA") without the depth needed for the audit. Push students to ask: "What evidence does Manus provide for this claim?" If there is no evidence cited, the claim needs to be verified manually. The debrief is a critical evaluation exercise. Circulate and listen for students who are taking the Manus output at face value without interrogating it.
Website overview

Manual review

Visit hnry.co.nz directly and evaluate criterion 9: ethical considerations.
Cookie transparency
Is the cookie consent banner clear about what is being collected and why? Is it easy to decline non-essential cookies? Does the site honour that choice?
Data collection disclosure
Is the privacy policy findable and written in plain language? Are the purposes for data collection clearly stated? Is consent meaningful or coercive?
Accessibility equity
Beyond WCAG compliance: does the site make a genuine effort to serve users with access needs? Are there meaningful accommodations, or is accessibility a checkbox?
Note on scope: The audit focuses on the NZ site (hnry.co.nz). If you notice meaningful structural differences between the NZ, AU, and UK sites while browsing, these are worth recording as observations even if they are not required by the template.
Give students 4–5 minutes to visit the live site and evaluate these three areas. The ethical considerations criterion is often the weakest in student audits because it is the hardest to assess rigorously. Common weak responses: "Hnry has a cookie banner" (that is not an evaluation) or "the privacy policy is available" (same problem). Push students to make a judgement: is the practice good, adequate, or poor? And why? The difference between WCAG compliance (which automated tools like Lighthouse can check) and accessibility equity (which is a broader, more human question) is worth making explicit. A site can pass WCAG AA and still fail users with access needs if the accessible version is degraded or incomplete. Bridge to the next section: "Now that we have the on-site picture, let's look at the behavioural data — what actually happens when users arrive."
Section 3 — 20 minutes

Traffic analytics audit

Four GA4 reports, one analysis prompt, and a goal-metrics-benchmarks table to draft.
Transition into Section 3. Before advancing, confirm that all students have downloaded their four GA4 CSV exports. If anyone hasn't, they should download them now while you introduce the reports on the next slide. The four CSVs are: Traffic acquisition (session primary channel group), Pages and screens (page path), Events (event name), and Conversions (event name). Students who have not yet been granted GA4 access to the Hnry property should flag this now so it can be resolved before the analysis activity.
Traffic analytics

The four GA4 reports

01
Traffic acquisition
Session primary channel group
Where visitors are coming from: organic search, direct, paid social, paid search, referral, email, and other channels. This is the channel-level picture of how Hnry is being found.
02
Pages and screens
Page path and screen class
What content users are consuming: which pages receive the most traffic, how long users spend on them, and where engagement is high or low. Reveals what Hnry's audience actually looks at.
03
Events
Event name
What users are doing on the site: clicks, form interactions, video plays, scroll depth, and other tracked behaviours. This is the behavioural layer underneath the page view data.
04
Conversions
Event name
Which conversion events are being tracked and at what volume: sign-ups, free trial starts, contact form submissions, and any other actions Hnry has designated as conversion goals.
These four reports are your source data for the audit. The analysis prompt in the next activity will work across all four.
Walk through the four reports briefly. Key things to flag: (1) Traffic acquisition is the most strategically revealing for an audit because it shows the channel mix. A site over-indexed on direct traffic may have brand awareness but weak SEO; a site with high paid search spend relative to organic suggests an SEO gap. (2) Pages and screens often surprises students — the pages getting the most traffic are not always the homepage. Blog content or feature pages sometimes outperform the homepage for organic traffic. (3) Events require careful reading: a high event count for "scroll" means users are reading, but a high event count for an exit link means they're leaving for a competitor. (4) Conversions is the most important report for connecting analytics to business goals. If the conversion event count is low or zero, that is a significant finding. Ask students: "Before we look at the data — what conversion events do you expect Hnry to be tracking, given what you know about their business?"
Activity — Traffic analytics

Developing the analysis prompt

Before you run the analysis, build your prompt:
Develop a prompt that instructs your preferred LLM to analyse the four GA4 reports in the context of Hnry's current goals and objectives. The prompt should direct the LLM to extract strategic insights relevant to those goals, as well as any other patterns or findings the data surfaces that are useful for the audit.
What your prompt needs
  • Context: Hnry's business model and stated goals
  • Which four reports you are uploading and what each measures
  • Instruction to link findings back to goals, not just describe the data
  • Request for a goal-metrics-benchmarks summary table
Framing decisions to consider
  • What role are you assigning the LLM?
  • Are you asking for findings across all four reports or one at a time?
  • What format do you want the output in?
  • How specific are you being about Hnry's goals?
Draft your prompt, then share it with the group before running it. Discuss the framing decisions you made and what you are expecting to get back.
Give students 5 minutes to draft their prompt before the group share. This pre-run sharing step is important: it surfaces prompt design decisions before students see outputs, which prevents them from working backwards from a result. Good prompts will: (1) specify Hnry's goals explicitly (e.g., acquiring new sole trader clients, reducing financial admin friction, growing in the NZ market); (2) name each of the four reports and describe what they measure; (3) ask for strategic framing, not just data description; (4) request the goal-metrics-benchmarks table in a specified format. Weak prompts will say "analyse these CSV files and tell me about the traffic" without any Hnry context. The goal-metrics-benchmarks table is a required audit template output: goal (what Hnry is trying to achieve), metric (the GA4 measure that tracks it), benchmark (industry average or previous period comparison).
Activity — Traffic analytics

Running the analysis and reviewing the output

  • Upload the CSVs and run your prompt
    Upload the four GA4 exports to your LLM and run your analysis prompt. Use a model with file upload capability (ChatGPT, Claude, or Gemini with file support).
  • Review the output critically
    Where does the AI interpretation hold up? Where is it superficial, incorrect, or missing context that you can supply? Mark these gaps directly in your audit template notes.
  • Draft the goal-metrics-benchmarks table
    Using the LLM output as a starting point, draft the required table. Verify each metric and benchmark value against the actual CSV data. Do not take the LLM's numbers on trust.
  • Identify key insights
    What are the two or three most strategically significant findings from this data? These should drive your analytics audit section, not a list of every metric the LLM mentioned.
Give students 7–8 minutes. Step 3 is the most important and the most commonly skipped: students trust the LLM's numbers without checking them against the source CSV. This is a critical analysis failure. If the LLM states that organic search accounts for 42% of traffic, that number should be verifiable in the traffic acquisition CSV. The goal-metrics-benchmarks table needs real numbers, not AI-generated estimates. For benchmarks: students may not have industry benchmarks available during the session. That is fine — they can use prior period comparisons (if available in the data) or note that a benchmark is needed. The two-or-three key insights instruction is deliberately restrictive: it forces students to prioritise rather than list everything, which is what the audit report structure requires. Common weak outputs: a list of every channel in the traffic report without any analysis of what the channel mix means for Hnry's strategy.
Section 4 — 20 minutes

Paid advertising

Analysing Hnry's Meta ad activity using the Meta Ads Library and connecting findings back to the traffic acquisition data.
Transition into Section 4. This section uses the Meta Ads Library, which is a free, publicly accessible tool that requires no login. Before starting, confirm that students know where to find Hnry New Zealand in the library. The correct search is "Hnry" with the country filter set to New Zealand. There may be multiple "Hnry" results; students should look for the verified advertiser page associated with the hnry.co.nz domain. Note: the Ads Library shows active ads. If Hnry has no active Meta ads at the time of this tutorial, students should note this as a finding in itself and look at the library's "All ads" filter to see historical activity.
Paid advertising

The Meta Ads Library

What it is
The Meta Ads Library is a public database of all active and recently active ads running on Facebook, Instagram, and the Meta network. It is the primary tool for auditing a brand's paid social activity without requiring ad account access.
Why it is useful for this audit
It shows what Hnry is currently spending creative effort on, what messages and formats they are testing, and how their paid strategy connects (or doesn't connect) to what the traffic data shows arriving from paid channels.
How to find Hnry NZ
Go to the Meta Ads Library (facebook.com/ads/library). Set the country to New Zealand and the ad category to All ads. Search "Hnry". Identify the advertiser page associated with hnry.co.nz and confirm you are viewing the correct entity.
If no active ads appear: check the "All ads" date filter. An absence of current activity is itself an audit finding. Note it.
This slide is primarily instructional. Walk students through finding Hnry in the library live if possible — project your screen and search together. Things to watch for: (1) Multiple "Hnry" results may appear. The correct one will show the hnry.co.nz URL and will have the verified badge. (2) The library shows the number of active ads and, for some regions, estimated spend ranges. (3) Students can filter by ad format (images, videos, etc.) to make the analysis in the next activity more structured. One observation worth making before the analysis: the Meta Ads Library is a transparency tool mandated by Meta to comply with advertising regulations in various jurisdictions. It is not designed for competitive research, but it functions very well as one. This is an example of a regulatory requirement creating a useful research resource.
Paid advertising

Four things to analyse

Use these four areas as the analytical frame for the activity on the next slide.
01 — Formats
What ad formats is Hnry using?
Single image, video, carousel, story, collection. Note which formats appear most frequently and whether the format mix suggests testing, a settled strategy, or limited creative investment.
02 — Message angles
What is the copy leading with?
The problems, benefits, or emotions the ad copy opens with. Is Hnry leading with pain (tax stress), aspiration (financial freedom), social proof (customer stories), or product features? What angle appears most consistently?
03 — Offer types
What is being promoted?
Free trial, feature announcement, social proof, seasonal promotion, content download, or brand awareness. Note whether offers are conversion-focused (direct response) or awareness-focused, and how the mix has shifted over time.
04 — Creative patterns
What recurring elements appear?
Visual styles, colour palettes, photography versus illustration, logo placement, text overlays. Recurring elements across multiple ads suggest a tested creative system; wide variation may suggest ongoing testing or inconsistent brand application.
This framework slide sets up the activity. Spend 2 minutes walking through the four areas before students start. The framing is deliberately analytical rather than descriptive: the goal is not to list what Hnry's ads look like but to interpret what their ad strategy reveals. For example: if all ads lead with pain messaging, that tells you something about Hnry's hypothesis about what motivates their audience. If all ads feature a free trial offer, that tells you Hnry believes trial conversion is their primary paid objective. These interpretations connect directly to the strategic layer of the audit. Note: students who have used this framework in other contexts (e.g., competitive advertising analysis in other papers) will recognise the structure. For students who haven't, the next slide gives them a guided prompt approach.
Activity — Paid advertising

Using an LLM to analyse Hnry's ads

Step 1: get the URL
Copy the Hnry New Zealand Meta Ads Library URL directly from your browser — the URL that shows Hnry's advertiser page with their active ads displayed.
Step 2: build your prompt
Paste the URL into your preferred LLM with web browsing enabled. Craft a prompt using the four analytical areas (formats, message angles, offer types, creative patterns) as the frame for the analysis.
What your prompt should request:
Ask the LLM to visit the URL, review the visible ads, and provide an analysis structured around the four areas from the previous slide. Include context about who Hnry is and what audience they are targeting. Ask for a summary judgement on the overall paid social strategy, not just a description of individual ads.
Review the output. Where is the analysis specific and evidenced? Where is it vague or inferred without direct evidence from the ads? Use your own viewing of the library to challenge or confirm the LLM's findings before drafting your audit section.
Give students 7–8 minutes. LLM web browsing quality varies significantly between models. Claude and ChatGPT with browsing enabled will generally access the Ads Library URL, but the library is JavaScript-rendered and some models struggle to extract structured ad data from it. If students find the LLM cannot access the URL meaningfully, an alternative approach is to screenshot a set of ads from the library and upload them as images with a prompt asking for visual and copy analysis across the four dimensions. The debrief question is the most important instruction on this slide: every finding the LLM makes should be verifiable by the student looking at the same ads. This is not a trust exercise; it is an analytical one. Students who draft their paid advertising audit section based entirely on LLM output without cross-checking it against the actual ads will produce a weaker piece of analysis. Common issue: LLMs often describe the most prominent/featured ads and miss smaller or older ads in the library. Ask students: "Did the LLM look at all the ads or just the most visible ones?"
Paid advertising

Connecting to your traffic data

Cross-reference what you found in the Meta Ads Library against the paid social and paid search rows in your GA4 traffic acquisition CSV.
Questions to ask
  • ›Does the paid social channel in GA4 show meaningful traffic? Does that match the level of ad activity you saw in the library?
  • ›Is paid search present in the traffic data? If Hnry is not running Google Ads, that absence is worth noting as a strategic choice.
  • ›Do the paid channels in GA4 convert at a different rate than organic channels? What does that tell you about the quality of paid traffic?
If paid search is absent
An absence of paid search in the traffic acquisition data is a strategic finding in its own right. It may mean Hnry is relying entirely on organic search, that their Google Ads activity is not tagged correctly for GA4, or that paid search spend is zero. Each explanation has different strategic implications worth noting in your audit.
This is a synthesis slide, not an activity slide. Give students 3–4 minutes to cross-reference their two data sources: the Meta Ads Library findings and the GA4 traffic acquisition CSV. The key analytical move is connecting the creative strategy visible in the ads library to the actual traffic and conversion outcomes visible in GA4. For example: if Hnry is running a lot of free trial ads on Meta but the paid social channel shows very low conversion events in GA4, there is a gap between the offer being promoted and the outcome being achieved. That gap is a finding. The paid search absence point is specifically mentioned in the brief because it is a common situation: many NZ SaaS brands run Meta ads but not Google Ads, relying on organic search to capture intent-based traffic. If that is the case for Hnry, it is worth analysing whether that is a deliberate strategy or a gap.

Close — 5 minutes

Bringing it all together

  • SEO reveals how Hnry is found in search: what it ranks for, how strong its authority is, and where competitors are outperforming it.
  • Website overview evaluates where users land: whether the experience, design, and ethics meet the standard the audience expects.
  • Traffic analytics shows what actually happens when users arrive: which channels deliver, which pages engage, and whether conversion goals are being met.
  • Paid advertising shows the investment layered on top: what Hnry is actively paying to promote and whether it connects to the organic and conversion picture.
Section 5: social media
Not covered today. Complete the social media audit independently using Rival IQ. This section follows the same structure: identify the data, use AI to assist the analysis, apply your own critical judgement.
Before next tutorial
Aim to have a working draft of all five audit sections complete. Check the course outline for the Assignment 2 due date. Bring any unresolved analytical questions to the next tutorial.
Close the session by connecting the four sections explicitly. The key message: the four sections are not four separate tasks to tick off — they are four layers of the same story. A strong audit will draw connections between them. For example: "SEMrush shows Hnry ranks well for branded terms but weakly for non-branded acquisition terms. The traffic data confirms that direct and organic branded traffic dominate. The paid ads show Hnry investing in Meta to reach cold audiences, which the traffic data suggests is generating some paid social sessions but low conversion. Together, these findings point to a specific strategic challenge: Hnry has strong retention of known users but weak acquisition of new audiences through non-paid, non-branded channels." That synthesis is the audit. Re: social media audit — students often underestimate how long Section 5 takes. Rival IQ needs to have been configured in advance. If students haven't set it up yet, they should do so this week. Close by confirming the Assignment 2 due date from the course outline and asking: "What is one thing you still need to do before your audit is ready to write up?" Take a few responses to surface common gaps.
